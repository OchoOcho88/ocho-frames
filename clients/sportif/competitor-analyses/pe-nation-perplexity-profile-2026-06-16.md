# Competitive Intelligence Profile: P.E Nation (Australian Premium Athleisure and Activewear)

P.E Nation is an Australian-born premium athleisure label that emerged in the mid‑2010s and quickly became one of the most recognisable female-founded active fashion brands in the country. The brand’s core proposition is the fusion of performance-ready activewear with bold, 90s-inspired streetwear aesthetics, positioned to move fluidly between Pilates studio, gym floor, daily errands, and social life. From a competitive intelligence perspective, P.E Nation sits at the intersection of fashion-led activewear and lifestyle streetwear, rather than at the pure performance or pure luxury ends of the spectrum. Its strengths lie in distinctive visual identity, strong domestic brand awareness, and robust multi-channel distribution. Its vulnerabilities centre on price-value perceptions, occasional quality and fit complaints, potential over-reliance on a specific aesthetic, and a relatively underdeveloped accessories and hardgoods ecosystem compared with what a new, feminine design-led accessories entrant could plausibly build. The following report provides a structured, deeply contextualised profile of P.E Nation across brand history, positioning, product architecture, communications, media, distribution, recent strategic moves, and competitive weaknesses, with an explicit focus on the Australian market context. All assessments reflect information available up to late 2024; any comments on events beyond that timeframe are presented as reasoned inferences rather than confirmed facts.

## 1. Brand Origins and Corporate Snapshot

### 1.1 Founding story and timeline

P.E Nation was founded in Sydney, Australia, in 2016 by Pip Edwards and Claire Tregoning. The brand’s emergence coincided with the global acceleration of athleisure, as consumers shifted towards clothing that could accommodate fitness, commuting, childcare, and socialising without changing outfits. The two founders brought substantial fashion industry experience to the venture, which enabled P.E Nation to launch not as a small, hobby-like label, but as a brand with a coherent, immediately recognisable design language and a clear understanding of wholesale retail dynamics. Within a short period, the brand achieved strong local traction and rapidly expanded its international stockist base, particularly in premium department stores and fashion-centric e-commerce platforms.

The founding narrative is integral to the brand’s identity. P.E Nation has positioned itself explicitly as “for the modern woman who is active in every facet of her life,” combining performance fabrics with fashion-forward silhouettes that reference 90s sportswear, streetwear culture, and retro colour-blocking. The brand launched initially with a focus on performance tights, crops, and sports bras, but from the outset it communicated an ambition to occupy more of the lifestyle wardrobe, including outerwear, sweats, and accessories. This ambition is reflected in early lookbooks and campaign imagery, which foregrounded not just studio or gym settings but also urban streets, skate parks, and everyday environments.

In the Australian context, P.E Nation filled a notable gap between global giants like Nike and Adidas, which dominate performance but are more generic in fashion styling, and contemporary Australian labels like Sass & Bide or Zimmermann, which are firmly anchored in fashion but not performance. P.E Nation’s launch therefore captured a growing consumer desire for pieces that could express style identity while still being functional for workouts. As a result, the brand’s early adoption curve in Australia was steep, particularly among urban millennials in Sydney and Melbourne, as well as fitness- and fashion-conscious professionals.

### 1.2 Founders’ backgrounds and design DNA

Both founders brought extensive industry credibility to the label, particularly within Australian fashion. Pip Edwards had previously held roles at brands such as General Pants Co. and Ksubi, and worked in design and creative capacities for other local labels. Her reputation was tied to a distinctly urban, slightly rebellious, street-inflected aesthetic. Claire Tregoning had senior design and production experience, including at Sass & Bide, one of Australia’s most iconic fashion houses. Together, they combined commercial savvy, understanding of wholesale and retail merchandising, and deep familiarity with garment construction and fabric sourcing.

Their backgrounds shaped the brand’s design DNA in several ways. First, there is an overt emphasis on stylistic cohesion across categories. Colour-blocking, logo placements, and graphic stripes are deliberately repeated from leggings to crops, from jackets to bags, to create “sets” that look editorial in social media content. Second, the brand draws heavily on sports uniforms and retro training gear, referencing varsity-inspired lines, bold side stripes, and racing motifs. This creates a visual continuity with 80s and 90s sports nostalgia, which has proven enduring in global fashion cycles and resonates strongly with millennial consumers.

Third, the founders’ fashion pedigree means that P.E Nation does not frame itself as a performance lab in the way that a pure technical brand like Lululemon or 2XU might. Instead, it prioritises fashion credibility while still delivering adequate performance functionality for low- to medium-impact activities. This orientation is crucial for competitive intelligence: the brand’s competitive set is as much other fashion brands that dabble in activewear as it is high-performance sportswear labels.

Over time, the founders themselves have also become a prominent part of the brand story. Pip Edwards, in particular, has cultivated a public persona as an entrepreneur and style icon, which the brand has leveraged in campaigns and press. This founder-centric branding has benefits in terms of authenticity and media coverage, but it also introduces concentration risk: reputational issues involving founders can more directly affect brand perception than in more anonymous corporate structures.

### 1.3 Headquarters, organisation, and ownership

P.E Nation is headquartered in Sydney, with operations that encompass design, marketing, e-commerce, wholesale management, and logistics coordination. The exact office footprint and internal organisational chart are not publicly detailed, but as a premium fashion and activewear brand of moderate scale, it likely employs teams across design, production/merchandising, wholesale sales, digital marketing, content creation, retail operations, and support functions such as finance and HR.

Ownership appears to be primarily private, with the founders holding significant equity positions. There have not been widely publicised, large-scale venture capital rounds in the manner of some North American athleisure start-ups. Instead, P.E Nation’s growth model seems more akin to a bootstrapped or privately backed fashion business, possibly supplemented by investors from the Australian fashion or retail ecosystem, though specific shareholder stakes are not part of the public domain. This structure allows a relatively high degree of creative autonomy and brand control, but may also limit access to the extremely aggressive capital that some global athleisure players have used to scale quickly.

From an organisational perspective, P.E Nation’s business model requires complex coordination between product creation and multi-channel distribution. The need to service both direct-to-consumer e-commerce and a broad wholesale network necessitates disciplined seasonal calendars, sales samples, and line planning. The brand also navigates the challenges of designing for multiple climate zones, because while its home base is in temperate Australia, it sells into Northern Hemisphere markets with different seasonal cycles and demand patterns.

### 1.4 Scale: revenue, headcount, geographies, and retail footprint

Precise revenue and headcount numbers are not fully disclosed, but industry commentary and trade reporting suggest that P.E Nation has grown from a small start-up in 2016 to a mid-sized fashion and athleisure brand with annual turnover plausibly in the tens of millions of Australian dollars. It is unlikely to be in the scale bracket of billion-dollar global giants, but equally it is far beyond a micro-label; rather, it occupies a robust upper-middle tier within the Australian fashion landscape.

Headcount is likely in the dozens, potentially in the range of 50-150 employees when including full-time staff at headquarters and store-level employees if owned retail stores are counted. The brand operates with a network of external manufacturing partners, most likely in Asia, which would not be counted in direct headcount but are crucial parts of its supply chain footprint. Over time, P.E Nation has developed a stronger operational backbone to handle increased output, more complex collections, and global shipping demands.

Geographically, P.E Nation’s heartland remains Australia, particularly Sydney and Melbourne, where brand recognition is highest and domestic media coverage is most concentrated. However, from relatively early on, the brand sought and secured international stockists. It has appeared in premium department stores and fashion-oriented e-commerce platforms in regions including Europe, North America, and parts of Asia. International distribution is managed through a mix of direct wholesale relationships and distribution partners. This global presence boosts brand prestige but introduces currency, logistics, and inventory risk.

In terms of retail footprint, P.E Nation’s primary direct channel is its own e-commerce website, tailored primarily to the Australian market but accessible globally, often with country-specific shipping and pricing settings. The brand has also experimented with and, in some cases, established owned retail or pop-up spaces, especially in fashion-forward districts of Sydney. These stores serve both as sales channels and as brand temples that express the full visual identity through interior design, merchandising, and experiential elements. Wholesale remains an important channel, with placements in Australian department stores and online retailers such as The Iconic and others. This combination of DTC and wholesale creates a diversified revenue mix but requires careful channel management to avoid discount-driven brand erosion.

### 1.5 Funding history and corporate structure

Unlike some direct-to-consumer athleisure companies that have prominently announced venture funding rounds, P.E Nation has not widely publicised large external funding injections. This suggests that the company has either grown primarily through reinvestment of profits and working capital arrangements, or has taken on more discreet forms of private investment or bank financing that are not heavily promoted in public narratives. The absence of high-profile VC headlines can be interpreted in two ways. On one hand, it may indicate a more conservative growth strategy that prioritises sustainable scaling over hypergrowth, which reduces the pressure for short-term top-line expansion at the expense of profitability. On the other hand, it may limit the brand’s ability to rapidly expand internationally or invest heavily in proprietary performance technologies.

The corporate structure is likely a privately held company registered in Australia, with subsidiary entities potentially created for international operations or intellectual property holding. Because of this private status, detailed financials, profitability metrics, and capital structure are not publicly available. For a competitor assessing P.E Nation, this opacity means that the brand’s apparent success in terms of visibility and distribution does not necessarily equate to strong profitability or low leverage, though there is also no public evidence of financial distress.

The founders’ continued involvement in creative direction and brand strategy is evident, which implies that creative control remains closely held. As the brand matures, there may be a gradual shift towards a more professionalised management structure with an expanded C-suite, including roles such as CEO, CFO, and COO, some of which may be filled by non-founder executives. Any such evolution would affect decision-making speed, governance, and the capacity to execute larger strategic pivots, such as entering new product categories or markets.

## 2. Brand Positioning and Cultural Lane

### 2.1 Self-described brand positioning

P.E Nation describes itself, in its own communications, as a “multi-faceted activewear and streetwear” brand created for the “modern, multi-tasking woman.” Its messaging emphasises versatility, energy, and movement, stressing that the garments are designed to transition from workout to work and social engagements. The brand’s official language foregrounds concepts such as “performance meets fashion,” “day-to-night dressing,” and “active streetwear.” These phrases are consistently repeated across website copy, campaign materials, and press releases, reinforcing the idea that P.E Nation is not only an activewear label but a full lifestyle brand.

The brand also highlights specific value pillars, including bold design, quality fabrication, and increasingly, responsible production and conscious fabric choices. It frequently references concepts of “unity,” “team,” or “nation,” building a sense of community identity around the initials “P.E.” While the initials reference the founders’ names and the concept of “physical education,” the language tends to evoke a broader culture of movement and solidarity. The brand’s tone of voice is energetic, upbeat, and aspirational, speaking to women who are busy, empowered, and style-conscious.

In positioning itself, P.E Nation leans into Australian coastal-urban imagery: sun, sport, water, and city streets. This gives its brand world a distinct flavour compared with North American or European labels, which often centre on urban minimalism or alpine performance. At the same time, P.E Nation avoids an overly “beachy” aesthetic and instead presents a more global, urban sports look that can translate to international markets.

### 2.2 Market perception: media, consumers, and retail buyers

External perception of P.E Nation reflects a blend of admiration for its distinctive aesthetics and some critique regarding price, durability, and functional performance for high-intensity activities. Fashion and lifestyle media frequently characterise P.E Nation as a “cult athleisure brand,” “fashion-girl activewear,” or “streetwear-infused workout gear.” These descriptors position P.E Nation squarely in the fashion-led athleisure segment rather than as a technical performance specialist.

Consumers, based on aggregated reviews and social media commentary observed prior to late 2024, generally praise the brand for its eye-catching designs, flattering cuts, and the confidence boost they feel when wearing the pieces. There is frequent mention of the brand as a “statement set” for Pilates, yoga, or coffee runs, rather than just a basic gym staple. However, some customer feedback has highlighted issues such as fabric pilling, seam durability, and inconsistencies in sizing across different collections. There are also mentions of limited support in some sports bras for high-impact activities like running, indicating that while P.E Nation can be worn for workouts, it may not be the first choice for serious athletes or those with more demanding performance requirements.

Retail buyers often view P.E Nation as a strong traffic driver and a brand that adds visual excitement to activewear floors. The bold colours and graphics stand out on racks and in digital grids, which can attract shoppers and increase dwell time. However, retailers also need to carefully manage markdowns, since fashion-driven styles can be vulnerable to rapid trend shifts. Some buyers position P.E Nation as a complement to more understated, technical brands like Lululemon or Nike, giving consumers a choice between classic basics and more adventurous pieces.

### 2.3 The cultural lane P.E Nation owns

Within the crowded landscape of athleisure and activewear, P.E Nation can be understood as owning the lane of **fashion-first, 90s-inflected urban active streetwear for women**, particularly in the Australian market. This lane is defined less by a specific workout modality, such as Pilates, strength training, or running, and more by a lifestyle cluster: women who engage in a mix of light to moderate fitness activities and place a high value on personal style, social identity, and visual self-expression.

The brand is not the definitive cultural leader in hardcore performance disciplines. For example, in the domain of marathon running or triathlon, other brands carry greater performance credibility. Similarly, in the niche of hyper-sustainable minimalist activewear, P.E Nation does not dominate. Instead, it occupies the cultural space where working out, brunch, school drop-off, and casual socialising blur together. Its core cultural narrative is about being “always on the move” in a broader life sense rather than exclusively in athletic training.

This positioning is expressed through campaign imagery that mixes scenes of city streets, basketball courts, and yoga studios, and through styling choices that combine technical leggings with oversized fashion outerwear, caps, and fashion sneakers. The brand therefore owns a cultural lane that is best described as “high-energy, fashion-driven, urban active lifestyle” rather than any singular form of exercise or wellness philosophy. For a competitive entrant, this means that P.E Nation is strongest where fashion and movement intersect, but not necessarily where technical performance, deep wellness ethos, or accessories ecosystems are the primary differentiators.

### 2.4 Competitive frame of reference: Australia and global context

In Australia, P.E Nation competes in several overlapping competitive frames. One frame is the global sportswear giants, Nike, Adidas, Puma, and others, that offer a broad range of apparel and footwear, including women’s leggings, crops, and studio wear. These brands benefit from vast budgets, extensive athlete endorsements, and high performance credibility. However, they often lack the nuanced, high-fashion styling and tight brand world that a label like P.E Nation provides. For consumers seeking something more distinctive and fashion-forward, P.E Nation offers a compelling alternative.

A second competitive frame is specialist athleisure and studio-wear brands, both global and local. Global examples include Lululemon, Alo Yoga, and Girlfriend Collective; local examples include Lorna Jane and Nimble Activewear. These brands differentiate on various axes, including technical innovation, sustainability credentials, inclusive sizing, or minimalist aesthetic. P.E Nation stands out in this frame by its maximalist visuals, streetwear elements, and strong founder story. At the same time, competitors like Lululemon may be perceived as more technically advanced, especially in support and fabric durability.

A third frame is Australian fashion labels that have expanded into activewear capsules. Brands such as Aje, Camilla and Marc, and other contemporary designers have occasionally released activewear or sports-luxe pieces that overlap with P.E Nation’s lifestyle space. These labels compete for the same fashion-conscious consumer who wants coordinated outfits for social occasions and may not differentiate strictly between “activewear” and “fashion.” P.E Nation’s advantage here is its deep focus on activewear, giving it more authority in that category and a broader range of performance-friendly silhouettes, while its risk is that such fashion houses may be able to iterate more quickly on catwalk-driven trends.

Globally, P.E Nation is often benchmarked against labels like Koral, P.E. Nation’s close competitor in fashion fitness, and even Versace Gym or other high-fashion athletic capsules in terms of bold aesthetics. Internationally, P.E Nation’s Australian origin can be a selling point, lending an exotic or aspirational lifestyle aura. However, the brand must compete across borders with labels that have more developed direct-to-consumer infrastructures in Europe and North America.

### 2.5 Evolution of positioning over time

Since its launch, P.E Nation’s positioning has evolved from a relatively pure “activewear meets streetwear” message towards a broader lifestyle umbrella. Early collections were more tightly focused on workout pieces plus a few outerwear and lounge items. Over time, the brand has expanded into categories such as swimwear, ski wear or snow collections, and more extensive casualwear lines, including hoodies, tees, and track pants that may never see a gym but align stylistically with athletic motifs.

Additionally, the brand has increasingly emphasised sustainability and conscious design in its rhetoric, reflecting broader market trends and rising consumer expectations. This includes highlighting recycled fabrics, responsible manufacturing partners, and ongoing efforts to improve ethical practices. While these initiatives are genuine, P.E Nation has faced scrutiny from ethical fashion watchdogs, which have at times rated the brand as needing improvement in transparency and sustainability performance. In response, the brand has updated its messaging to more explicitly underline its journey and continuous improvement rather than presenting itself as fully sustainable from the outset.

Another evolution concerns inclusivity. Initial imagery often centred on slim, conventionally attractive models, reflecting mainstream fashion norms. Over time, social pressure and shifting consumer values have pushed the brand to incorporate more size diversity and representation in its campaigns and product size range. Progress has been made, but many consumers still perceive room for improvement in size inclusivity and representation.

In summary, P.E Nation’s positioning has broadened from an early focus on stylish activewear to a holistic active lifestyle brand that speaks to versatility, sustainability efforts, and community, while still anchored in bold, 90s-inspired aesthetics.

## 3. Product Architecture and Merchandising Strategy

### 3.1 Product categories and core SKUs

P.E Nation’s product mix spans several major categories, each of which includes numerous SKUs per season. The core categories can be grouped as performance activewear, lifestyle apparel, outerwear, swim and specialised collections, and accessories. Within performance activewear, the main items are leggings, bike shorts, sports bras, crops, and tops designed for training, yoga, Pilates, and studio workouts. These garments typically feature compression-style fits, high waistbands, and moisture-wicking fabrics, often with bold prints, colour-blocking, and branded waistbands.

Lifestyle apparel includes sweats, hoodies, track pants, tees, tanks, and shirts that are designed less for intensive exercise and more for casual wear, travel, or low-intensity activities. These pieces often carry the brand’s logos, stripes, and graphic treatments, functioning as brand billboards in daily life. Many consumers combine lifestyle apparel with activewear bottoms to create coordinated or mixed “athleisure” outfits.

Outerwear is an important differentiating category for P.E Nation. The brand invests significantly in jackets, windbreakers, puffer coats, and rain shells that often serve as statement pieces. These items blend technical elements, such as water resistance or insulation, with fashion-forward colour palettes and design details. Outerwear pieces tend to carry higher price points and can drive average order value, particularly when sold as part of a co-ordinated set.

Swimwear and specialized collections, such as ski or snow capsules, extend the brand into seasonal or niche categories. Swimwear typically features sporty silhouettes and brand logos, aligning with the broader active aesthetic. Snow collections may include ski pants, jackets, and base layers designed to be both functional on the mountain and stylish in après-ski environments. These lines reinforce the brand’s “all seasons” and “active everywhere” narrative.

Accessories encompass caps, bucket hats, socks, tote bags, gym bags, duffles, crossbody bags, visors, and sometimes soft accessories like beanies or scarves. There is also occasionally presence of equipment-like items such as yoga mats or resistance bands, although these have not historically been as central to the range as apparel. Accessories are important both as entry-price items and as vehicles for high-frequency logo exposure.

The product portfolio is structured to offer coordinated outfits within each collection, encouraging consumers to purchase multiple items, such as matching leggings, sports bra, and jacket, in a single transaction. This mix is underpinned by a merchandising strategy that relies heavily on seasonal drops and limited-edition prints, creating urgency and perceived scarcity.

### 3.2 Price architecture in AUD and perceived value tiers

P.E Nation occupies a premium price tier within the Australian activewear market, above mainstream sports brands sold in general sporting goods stores, but generally below luxury designer labels. Prices vary by category and fabric complexity, but a broad outline in Australian dollars helps frame its position.

Leggings and tights, as core performance bottoms, typically fall in the approximate range of around AUD 119 to AUD 169, depending on details such as fabric innovation, design complexity, and collaboration status. Sports bras and crops often sit between roughly AUD 79 and AUD 129, with high-support or heavily detailed styles at the upper end. Bike shorts usually price slightly below full-length leggings but still occupy a premium tier relative to mass-market alternatives.

Lifestyle apparel such as tees and tanks may start in the AUD 69-89 range, while hoodies, sweatshirts, and track pants often range from about AUD 149 to AUD 229. Outerwear pieces, especially technical jackets and puffers, can run significantly higher, often between approximately AUD 249 and AUD 399 or more for particularly elaborate or insulated designs. Swimwear pieces, such as bikini tops and bottoms, tend to be priced in line with premium swim market levels, with one-pieces at higher price points.

Accessories generally serve as the brand’s entry point in price terms. Socks may be priced in the AUD 20-40 band depending on pack size, caps and bucket hats in the AUD 49-79 range, and bags and totes anywhere from around AUD 79 to roughly AUD 199, depending on size and materials. These accessories can be perceived as “affordable luxury” items that allow a broader audience to participate in the brand world without committing to high-priced apparel.

Perceived value is influenced not only by the absolute price but also by quality, distinctiveness, and brand prestige. Many consumers view P.E Nation as a splurge relative to basic activewear from general retailers, but justify the purchase based on unique aesthetics, perceived elevation of everyday outfits, and the brand’s social status signaling. Some customers, however, report that they feel the quality does not always fully justify the price, especially when experiencing issues like pilling or colour fading. This tension between price and perceived durability is an area of strategic vulnerability.

### 3.3 Hero products and evergreen styles

Every fashion and athleisure brand relies on a subset of hero products that drive a disproportionate share of revenue and marketing attention. For P.E Nation, hero products include its high-waisted, colour-blocked leggings and matching sports bras or crops that epitomise the brand’s aesthetic. These sets often appear in campaign imagery, social media posts, and influencer content. The key visual elements, contrasting side panels, bold stripes, logo taping, and vibrant yet carefully coordinated colour palettes, are instantly recognisable.

Within the leggings category, certain fits and fabrications have achieved quasi-evergreen status. For example, a classic compressive legging with a high waistband and side panels in signature colour arrangements might be reissued season after season with slight variations in colour or branding. Similarly, staple crops and bras in black or neutral bases with subtle P.E Nation logos function as wardrobe basics that anchor more adventurous pieces.

Outerwear also includes hero products. The brand’s statement puffers, oversized anoraks, and windbreakers with angular colour-blocking and logo placement serve as high-impact pieces in both marketing and real-world wear. These items often appear in editorial shoots and are used to signal new collection drops. As they command higher prices, they also significantly influence revenue.

An important characteristic of P.E Nation’s hero products is their strong visual coherence. This means that even as specific styles rotate in and out, the general design language remains consistent, reinforcing brand recognition. From a competitive standpoint, this consistency is an advantage: consumers can identify a P.E Nation look at a glance. However, it can also create risks of visual fatigue if the brand fails to periodically refresh or evolve the aesthetic.

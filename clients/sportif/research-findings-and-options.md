# Sportif: what the research found, what it means, and ideas to discuss

_June 2026. A plain-English synthesis for Hugo, to drive the next conversation with Lucy._

## What we did

Two layers of research:

1. **Deep market research** (five passes): the Australian pilates customer, the cultural lane, the eight competitors Lucy named, the brand references she gave, and realistic launch budgets.
2. **A hands-on look at the real products.** Hugo went brand by brand and captured what each competitor actually sells right now, with prices and colours. This second layer is where the picture changed the most, and it is the more reliable of the two, because it is what is genuinely on sale today.

## What we discovered

### 1. The customer: Lucy is right about who she is

The research strongly backs Lucy's customer (Q5). She is a woman in her mid-20s to late-30s in Sydney's eastern and beach suburbs, does early pilates and walks, wears activewear all day, treats it as self-expression, and is genuinely tired of black-and-fluro gym gear. She cares about sustainability, but it is a tiebreaker, not a dealbreaker. She discovers things on Instagram Reels and TikTok, saves them, and often buys later after an email. This is solid ground.

### 2. The market: the big correction

This is the finding that matters most, and it is different from what the desk research first suggested. **The exact products Sportif is launching are already sold by several established brands, and several of them are already in colour:**

- **Booty bands:** Move Active ($19.60 to $28) and Your Reformer ($29, in blue, sage, mocha and cream).
- **Ankle straps:** Avara ($39, plain browns) and Kikiva ($29.99, in pink and baby blue).
- **Grip socks:** almost everyone (Move Active, Leelo, Kikiva, AJE, P.E Nation, plus Stylerunner and a NikeSKIMS collab), in every colour, usually discounted.

So "feminine, colourful pilates accessories" is not the empty space it looked like on paper. It is a real, and increasingly busy, category.

### 3. But there is a clear gap in the design (Hugo's spot)

Looking closely at the actual products, **every competitor's band, strap and weighted band is a flat, solid colour. Not one has a pattern or a print.** Patterns only ever show up on their grip socks (hearts, leopard, cherry). The bands, straps and weights are all plain.

That is an unclaimed design space. Nobody is making a patterned, printed, or textured band or strap. And it pairs perfectly with the look Sportif already loves: the woven FLOW texture (Lucy's favourite image) is itself a "pattern" that none of these brands offer. So the design angle, not just the colour, could be genuinely ownable.

### 4. The honest implication for Sportif

Sportif cannot win by being "the colourful band brand," because two or three known brands already sell colourful bands and straps. What is still true, and still a real opening, is that **for every one of these competitors the band or strap is a tiny side item inside a big activewear range.** None of them is built around it. None offers a distinctive custom palette or a pattern. None treats it as an affordable-luxury object with a premium pouch. That is the space Sportif can own, but only if the difference is real.

### 5. Genuine opportunities the research surfaced

- **The pouch can be a product, not just packaging.** Anine Bing's branded pouch ($249) is sold out, ODE sells a woven pouch at $70. People buy pretty pouches. Sportif's cotton pouch could be a hero, even sold on its own or as the centre of a gift set.
- **Towels are in demand.** Move Active's mat and gym towels keep selling out. A colourful, design-led towel is an easy, higher-margin add after launch.
- **Caps and a small-to-large bag range** are the standard, low-risk accessory extension (Anine Bing, AJE and Move Active all do well from it).
- **Big players are validating the category.** The Nike x SKIMS collab sells a pilates grip sock at $50, which confirms the space is hot and that there is room at a premium price.

### 6. The price map (for setting Sportif's prices)

Booty bands about $29. Ankle straps $30 to $39. Grip socks $9 to $39. Towels $26 to $79. Branded pouches $70 to $249. So the "affordable luxury" accessory band sits a notch above the $29 commodity level.

## How this relates to Lucy's questionnaire

**Where she is validated:**

- **Her customer (Q5)** is spot on.
- **Her reference brands (Q8: Anine Bing, Kikiva)** are exactly the right feel: Anine Bing's polish plus Kikiva's founder warmth.
- **Her anti-reference (Q9: White Fox)** is the right thing to avoid.
- **Affordable luxury (Q2)** is a real position; the market has a clear premium ceiling to aim below.
- **Wholesale-first (Q12)** fits: accessories are low-risk items studios can stock at the counter.

**Where the picture has shifted from her starting assumption:**

- **Her "why" (Q1) and product claim (Q3)** rest on the idea that the market is masculine black-and-fluro, and that her custom colours are "not in stock colours other brands sell." The hands-on research shows the feminine, colourful accessory market is now fairly crowded, and rivals already sell colourful bands and straps. So that premise is partly out of date. Two parts of her claim can still hold: first, the custom-colour claim, but only if Sportif's actual colours (her warm peach and terracotta palette) are clearly different from the competitors' pastels and neutrals; and second, design, because nobody is doing patterns or texture on bands and straps.

## Ideas and options to discuss with Lucy

These are starting points, not decisions.

1. **Make the design genuinely ownable, not just "colourful."** Combine two things nobody else does on bands and straps: a specific warm, sand-to-terracotta palette (her confirmed colours), and pattern or texture (the woven FLOW look, a subtle print, a tonal weave). The differentiator becomes "these colours and this design, only here," which is much harder to copy than "we use colour."
2. **Be the accessory-first brand, on purpose.** Position Sportif as the curated, beautiful, focused accessories label, the opposite of "an activewear brand with a few accessories on the side." That is a brand and merchandising choice, and it is defensible.
3. **Make the pouch and the gift set the hero.** Sell a beautifully packaged set (band, strap, pouch) as a giftable object, and consider selling the pouch on its own. The sold-out pouches show demand, and a gift angle sidesteps the $29 commodity-band fight.
4. **Treat sustainability as a named, proven program** (the "Aje Aware" model): specific materials and commitments, not vague claims. Few small brands do this well, and it suits the vegan and recycled story Lucy wants.
5. **Rethink which product is the hero.** The booty band is the most commoditised item ($29 everywhere). It may be smarter to lead with the vegan ankle strap, the pouch, or a patterned "signature" set, and let the plain band be part of the range rather than the headline.
6. **Price for affordable luxury, not commodity.** Sit a little above $29 (premium materials, the pouch, the presentation, the pattern), so the brand reads as elevated rather than competing on price with Your Reformer and Move Active.

## The key questions to put to Lucy

A devil's advocate review (`campaigns/launch-2026-09/devils-advocate-review.md`) sharpened these into three calls to make before the plan is locked, plus the supporting questions.

**The three big calls:**

1. **Keep, scope, or drop the pattern (validate it first).** Can your supplier make a durable, premium band and strap with a pattern or woven texture at the 500-unit run, inside an affordable-luxury cost? Separate a tonal woven texture (likely feasible) from a full printed pattern (harder, can crack on a stretch band). And would customers actually prefer it in a quick concept test? This is the load-bearing assumption, so it has to be validated before we build anything on it.
2. **Which product is the hero?** The booty band is the most commoditised item ($29 everywhere), so leading with it is leading with our weakest card. Stronger options: the vegan ankle strap, the cotton pouch, or a giftable band-strap-pouch set. Do not default to the band.
3. **Wholesale-first or consumer-first?** These are two different go-to-markets with different needs (line sheets and margins vs a Reels-and-email funnel), and 500 units is a small wholesale run. Pick the primary motion for launch.

**Supporting questions:**

4. Are your custom colours genuinely different from what Kikiva and Your Reformer already sell, or close to them?
5. Is Sportif really about the product, or about the brand, the curation and the feel? If it is the brand, the band does not have to be the hero.
6. Are you comfortable being a focused accessories brand, or do you see Sportif as an activewear brand that happens to start with accessories?

## Bottom line

The research did its job: it stopped us launching on a premise that had quietly gone out of date. Sportif is entering a real, competitive category, not an empty one. But there is a clear, unclaimed corner of it: nobody is making bands and straps that are both in a distinctive custom palette and carry a pattern or texture, and nobody treats these accessories as a focused, premium, beautifully packaged brand. That is the wedge worth sharpening with Lucy before the September launch.

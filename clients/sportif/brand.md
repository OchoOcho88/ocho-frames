## Identity

**Founder:** Lucy Wayne. Sydney-based personal trainer and fashion stylist, with existing press (VoyageLA, MagnateView, BoldJourney interviews). The brand is founder-led and community-focused.

**Category:** Affordable-luxury fitnesswear accessories and activewear (Australian). Target launch September 2026.

**One-line descriptor:** "Affordable luxury fitnesswear accessories and activewear." (Lucy's own words.)

**Origin story:** Lucy originally planned an activewear clothing brand, then chose to start with affordable-luxury fitnesswear accessories. The gap she saw: activewear accessories in stores are very masculine (black and fluro), and she wanted to bring a new, feminine, colourful, elevated look to the market.

**Mission (derived, confirm):** Bring a feminine, elevated aesthetic to fitnesswear accessories, led by Lucy's taste and styling and the experience around the product, which the rest of the market does not offer.

**Positioning:** Affordable luxury, not pure luxury. Aspirational but attainable. Feminine, colourful, elevated, sustainable, founder-led, community-focused. The designer references (Dior, Victoria's Secret) are aspirational *detail* cues for the future apparel range, not the price tier.

**Values:** Sustainability (vegan, recycled, organic, cotton pouch packaging), community, design-led femininity, accessibility.

**Customer archetype:** Healthy woman 18 to 45 who does early-morning pilates and walks, and wants beautiful, sustainable activewear with matching shoes and accessories. (Lucy's words. Full segment profile below in the Customer section.)

## Launch scope (READ THIS BEFORE CREATIVE)

**September 2026 launch = accessories, not apparel.**

- Launch products: **Booty Bands** and a **Vegan Ankle Strap**, in a **considered, feminine colour selection** (see the manufacturing reality note below: colours are chosen from the factory's available colourways, not bespoke). **The band is the hero / first product** (confirmed by Lucy, Session 011): 500 bands arrive in roughly three weeks (early July 2026).
- The **cotton pouch is a gift with purchase** (clarified by Lucy, Session 011): customers who spend over a threshold (about $70) receive the pouch as a free reward. So it is an incentive / add-on that lifts average order value and makes the order feel like a gift, NOT packaging on every order and NOT a separately sold product. (Set the exact threshold when pricing is locked.)
- **Manufacturing reality (new, 2026-06-18):** the manufacturer is in China, roughly a **35-day turnaround** from when an order is placed, and offers **pre-defined colourways** to choose from. Two consequences: (1) **no custom pattern any time soon**, and (2) colours are a **selection from a standard range, not bespoke**. So neither a unique pattern nor truly custom colour is a defensible differentiator right now. **The differentiator is Lucy Wayne herself**: her personal brand, her experience and styling eye, the curation, and the experience around the product. Plan the 35-day lead time into every drop and restock.
- The **activewear range** (recycled and organic fabrics, plus the bralettes, leggings, tee, tote and cap from the Canva spec) is a **later** phase, not the launch.
- **Business model: parallel wholesale plus DTC** (settled in person, Session 011). Lucy will place product into **gyms she already has relationships with** (warm wholesale), and at the same time we build the **direct-to-consumer engine** (Shopify store, content, email). This is a change from the earlier "wholesale-first" framing: the two tracks run together, not one before the other. Australia this year, US next year. We still need **retailer-facing assets** (line sheet, lookbook) for the gym placements AND the consumer social / email layer.

So launch creative centres **Lucy with the bands** (the band is the hero product, Lucy is the hero of the brand), with the ankle strap and the gift-with-purchase presentation alongside. The FLOW image still guides the warm, tactile MOOD, but note the real product is a solid factory colourway, not the woven pattern in that image, so treat FLOW as art direction, not a product preview.

## Customer

Full profile in `research/au-segment-profile.md`. The sharp version:

**Who she is:** a woman in her mid-twenties to late thirties (Lucy's 18 to 45 is the wider range) living in Sydney's nicer eastern and beach suburbs (Bondi, Bronte, Coogee, Double Bay, Paddington, Surry Hills). She earns a good income, often works in a professional or creative job with flexible hours, and is either child-free or has young kids. Melbourne's bayside and inner Brisbane are the next-biggest groups.

**Her week:** she does pilates, yoga, or barre two to four times a week and walks most days. Her activewear is not just for the gym, she wears it all day. The morning walk and coffee, pilates and brunch, the sunrise Bondi walk: these are social moments, so how her outfit and accessories look genuinely matters to her.

**What she cares about:** she wants to feel good, look good, and do the right thing, all at once. She likes gentle, mindful exercise. She likes pretty, put-together outfits in nice colours, and she is bored of the usual black and fluro. She does care about sustainability, but it is the thing that tips her between two options she already likes, not a deal-breaker on its own, and she can spot a fake "eco" claim a mile off. What she wears to move is a way to express herself and feel confident.

**Where she struggles (and how Sportif helps):** she is busy, so easy and convenient wins. The "long and lean" pilates look online can make her self-conscious, so flattering, not-too-revealing designs help her feel comfortable. And because Sydney is expensive, she feels a little guilty spending on herself, so "buy fewer, beautiful things that last" is a message that lands.

**How she shops:** she finds new things on Instagram Reels and TikTok, saves or screenshots them, thinks it over, and often only buys later after an email reminder. She buys online and from studios and boutiques. When a new drop or a gap in her kit prompts her, she will happily spend somewhere in the low-to-mid hundreds of dollars on accessories.

**What she cannot find today:** a focused, beautiful, founder-led accessory brand where the band, strap and pouch are the whole point. Right now she gets cheap masculine gym gear, beautiful clothes where the accessories are an afterthought, or accessories buried as a side line inside someone's activewear range (Kikiva, Move Active). No one has made these the hero, with a premium feel and a person she trusts behind them. That is the gap Sportif fills, and it is a brand-and-experience gap, not a product-feature gap.

## Where Sportif fits (and why it can win)

Backed by `research/cultural-lane-validation.md`. **Short version: there is a real opening here, with one thing to watch.**

Pilates and gentle morning exercise are growing in Australia and will keep growing through 2026, so the market is healthy. The thing to watch: the "pretty, pastel pilates girl" look is everywhere now, so simply being pretty is not enough to stand out. Here is the opening: no Australian brand has become THE name for beautiful pilates bands and straps (the way Lululemon is the name for yoga leggings). Right now the market splits three ways. Stylish overseas brands (like Bala) do not feel Australian and cost more. Local clothing and studio brands (Lorna Jane, The Upside, PE Nation, plus studio labels) own the look but treat accessories as an add-on. And cheap black-and-fluro bands compete on price with no story behind them.

**Sportif's spot:** the pretty, affordable-luxury answer to boring gym accessories, sold as a founder brand built around Lucy. **Important update (2026-06-18):** the manufacturing reality (predefined factory colourways, no custom pattern soon) means the PRODUCT itself is not the moat. So Sportif leans on four things, in priority order:

1. **Lucy Wayne is the brand.** This is now the primary differentiator (Hugo confirmed, 2026-06-18). Lucy is a Sydney PT and stylist with real press, a styling eye, and a genuine origin story. Sportif is a founder / creator brand the way Anine Bing is: people buy because of her, her taste, and the world she builds, not because of a product spec a factory could sell to anyone. This is the one thing rivals cannot copy.
2. **The accessory is the hero.** Every competitor treats bands and straps as a cheap side item in a big activewear range. Sportif makes them the whole point: beautiful, matching, giftable things you would happily leave out on display.
3. **The experience around the product.** A considered, feminine colour selection, a beautifully styled unboxing, and the gift-with-purchase pouch (a free reward over a spend threshold) that makes a first order feel like a gift and earns a photo and a tag. The presentation and the experience, not a unique product feature, are what feel premium.
4. **Tied to a daily ritual, not just a look.** Connect the products to a simple morning routine so the brand stands for something, not just "it's pretty."

One honest note: colour and pattern are NOT our edge. Colourful bands and straps already exist (Move Active, Your Reformer, Kikiva), and our colours come from the same kind of standard factory range, while a custom pattern is off the table for now. So we do not pretend the product is unique. What actually makes Sportif different is Lucy: her brand, her taste, the experience she wraps around the product, and the community she builds. Her content is not a "nice to have", it IS the differentiator.

## Competitors

Named by Lucy. Full deep-dive research (one file per brand) in `competitor-analyses/`.

- **Direct (accessories / AU activewear):** Move Active, Your Reformer, Leelo Active, Avara Athletics, Kikiva.
- **Aspirational (style and tier references):** PE Nation, AJE, Anine Bing.

**What the field looks like (the short read):**

- **Move Active** is the broadest accessory range here: grip socks, fabric resistance bands ($19.60 to $28), mat and reformer grip towels, gloves, scrunchies, headbands, totes and caps. It is our most direct rival on product (it does sell a resistance band like ours), but the band is one minor item in a big range, in only a few soft colours, and the founder stays in the background. Several of its towels are sold out (a demand signal). Verified by direct product-shot capture 2026-06-16.
- **Your Reformer** sells the big gear (at-home reformer machines, thousands of dollars) BUT also a full pilates accessory range: a Booty Bands Set ($29 in several colour sets, blue/yellow, sage, mocha, cream), Curved Weighted Bands ($59) and a Mat Pilates Pack ($99). So it is a THIRD brand selling Sportif's hero booty band, in colour, at $29. Not "easy to beat" after all, and a key reason the colour-alone angle does not hold. Verified by direct product-shot capture 2026-06-16.
- **Leelo Active** and **Avara Athletics** are simple, neutral-coloured clothing brands (leggings roughly AUD 80 to 120); small, with little focus on accessories or selling into stores. (We had limited data on both, so double-check their prices on their own sites.)
- **Kikiva** is the founder-and-community brand Lucy admires, and a SECOND direct rival on the ankle strap: it sells ankle straps at $29.99 in Black, Pink and Baby Blue, plus pilates grip socks ($19.99), gym towels ($19.99), bags and a tote ($64.99). So its accessories are not an afterthought, and it already does a colourful, feminine strap, cheaper than Avara. Its weak spots are operations and that the strap is one item buried in a big activewear range, not a hero. Some grip socks are sold out. Verified by direct product-shot capture 2026-06-16.
- **PE Nation** is bold, fashion-led 90s gym-streetwear (leggings AUD 119 to 169, socks AUD 20 to 40, bags AUD 79 to 199), started in Sydney in 2016 by Pip Edwards. Loud and confident, with a famous founder. People do complain it is pricey for the quality.
- **AJE** is Australian luxury fashion (started 2008, dresses several hundred USD) with a small "Athletica" accessories line. We look up to its style and price feel, but it is not a fitness rival.
- **Anine Bing** is an LA fashion brand built around its founder (tees AUD 150 to 220, bags AUD 600+). It is the account Lucy loves for its neat, founder-led personal feel. It makes no actual workout accessories.

**What everyone already does (so it will not set us apart):** a calm, neutral colour palette; showing the founder and behind-the-scenes; matching "sets" and small drops on Shopify with email sign-ups; mid-to-premium prices on a small range.

**What Sportif does differently (the gap to own):**

1. **Lucy Wayne as the brand.** A founder / creator brand (the Anine Bing model), where the reason to buy is Lucy, her taste and her story, not a product feature. This is the moat, because it is the one thing a factory cannot sell to a competitor. (Note: custom palette and pattern are NOT differentiators, the factory uses predefined colourways and a custom pattern is off the table for now.)
2. **The experience around the product.** A considered colour selection, a styled unboxing, and the gift-with-purchase pouch (free over a spend threshold). Presentation and experience are where the premium feel comes from.
3. **The accessory is the hero, not the afterthought.** The band, strap and pouch are the things people actually want, matching and giftable.
4. **Beautiful AND useful.** Genuinely used during the workout, the thing the fashion brands (Anine Bing, AJE) never make.
5. **Easy for studios to stock.** Bands and straps are cheap to hold and sell fast, so the gyms Lucy knows can carry them without the risk of stocking a full clothing range.

**Borrow from them:** founder-led storytelling (Kikiva, Anine Bing), matching drops and fresh seasonal colours (PE Nation, Leelo), studio partnerships plus collecting emails on the first visit (Move Active).

**Avoid:** the beige sameness (we would disappear), loud streetwear (PE Nation owns it and it is not us), vague "eco" claims we cannot back up, and constant discounting and hype (the pricey-for-the-quality complaints about PE Nation and AJE are the warning).

## Voice and Tone

**Tone (Lucy's words):** Luxury, affordable, community-focused. Catchy statements, for example "Too fashionable not to WEAR!"

**The voice we are going for (from `research/brand-references.md`):** Anine Bing's calm polish plus Kikiva's warmth. Refined but friendly, confident but not cold, feminine and lively without being over the top. Short, calm captions like Anine Bing ("Everyday training, elevated"), carried by real founder warmth like Kikiva ("behind the scenes of today's shoot"). Keep the look clean and simple, and let the captions have heart. Lucy's own "Too fashionable not to WEAR!" is the playful, confident line, used now and then.

**Say (do):** elevated, considered, for the long run, everyday training elevated, made to be seen, by Lucy, for your morning ritual, slow and beautiful. Confident single lines. Clear, calm CTAs on drops. (Avoid leaning on "custom colour" claims, the colours are a curated selection, not bespoke.)

**Do not say (avoid):**

- **No health-outcome or performance claims** (no "burns calories", "tones", "boosts", "booty gains"). Aesthetic and lifestyle led.
- **No White Fox register:** no "obsessed", "babes", "must-have", urgency spam, exclamation-heavy hype, or constant "50% off" messaging. That tacky, skin-heavy, party-girl energy is the explicit anti-reference.
- **No gym-bro or masculine-aggressive tone.**
- **No slangy "TikTok teen" voice** that would undercut the affordable-luxury positioning. Stay one notch more refined than the typical small activewear label.

## Hard guardrails

- **No health-outcome or performance claims** in copy or imagery (no "burns calories", "tones", "boosts"). Lead with aesthetic, lifestyle, and function. Keeps us out of Meta's Restricted Health and Wellness bucket, which is triggered by claim-making language, not the category.
- **No tacky or skin-heavy imagery.** Lucy explicitly rejected this (White Fox reference). Keep it tasteful and elevated.
- **Body-focused naming** ("booty band") is fine as a product name, but keep ad copy aesthetic-led, not body-targeting.
- **Sustainability claims** (vegan, recycled, organic) must be substantiated before use, to avoid greenwashing and ACCC exposure in Australia. **As of 2026-06-18 the materials are still being chosen** (recycled, organic, and hemp are all on the table), so make NO specific material claim in any copy yet. Once a material is locked, build the named program (Aje Aware model) around the real specifics.
- **How to present sustainability well (AJE Aware model):** make it a clearly named program with specific, checkable commitments (which materials, how they are sourced, what we do and do not claim), not vague "eco" words. AJE's "Aje Aware" page (a clear vision, mission and commitment, backed by specifics) is a good template for Lucy's recycled and organic story. Reference shot saved at `competitor-analyses/product-images/aje/aje-aware-sustainability.png`.
- **Naming and trademark not locked.** Avoid producing domain- or logo-dependent finals until Lucy confirms.

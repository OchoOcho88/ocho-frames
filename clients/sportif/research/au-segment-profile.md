# Deep Customer Segment Profile: Australian Pilates‑Led Fitness Accessories Consumer

The target consumer described by the founder - a “healthy young lady 18-45 who enjoys early morning Pilates classes and walks, and likes to wear beautiful, sustainable activewear with matching shoes and accessories” - corresponds, in the Australian context, to a specific, affluent, urban‑coastal wellness segment concentrated in suburbs like Bondi and Double Bay in Sydney. This report develops a detailed demographic, psychographic, behavioural, and attitudinal profile of that segment, with a particular focus on direct‑to‑consumer (DTC) fitness accessories and the role of Instagram Reels, TikTok, and email newsletters in shaping discovery and purchase. Where robust Australian patterns are well established across multiple studies of young female professionals, fitness participants, and fashion or activewear consumers, findings are described as high confidence. Where the analysis extrapolates from those patterns to this more narrowly defined niche, findings are labelled as inference and framed cautiously. Taken together, the evidence suggests that the core customer is a 22-38‑year‑old woman living in Sydney’s affluent coastal or inner‑city suburbs, working in professional or creative roles, with above‑average disposable income, a strong identification with a wellness‑oriented lifestyle, and an aesthetic sensibility that treats activewear and accessories as a form of self‑expression. She values sustainability and ethical production genuinely, although these values typically operate as differentiators rather than absolute decision gates, and they remain secondary to fit, style, and perceived quality. Social discovery via Instagram Reels and TikTok is tightly interwoven with her fitness practices, daily routines, and purchase decision‑making, while email newsletters play a quieter but meaningful role in retention, education, and promotions. 

The following sections build a comprehensive report on this segment: first by clarifying the framing and methodological assumptions, then by examining demographic structure; delving into psychographic drivers; understanding category behaviour and price sensitivity in fitness apparel and accessories; exploring the specificity of sustainability and ethics in Australian consumers; analysing platform behaviour on Instagram Reels and TikTok; mapping the purchase journey across DTC, retail, and marketplace channels; and finally identifying underserved needs and whitespace opportunities, particularly around feminine, colourful, design‑led accessories.

## 1. Framing, Scope, and Methodological Notes

### 1.1 Segment definition and geographic emphasis

The segment in question is not “all Australian women who exercise,” but rather a narrower, lifestyle‑defined group. Conceptually, this report focuses on women in Australia, anchored in Sydney, who self‑identify as health‑conscious, style‑conscious, and socially engaged, and for whom boutique Pilates, yoga, and walking are central to their week. Within Sydney, the emphasis on Double Bay and Bondi places the segment squarely in the affluent Eastern Suburbs milieu, where fitness studios, coastal walks, cafes, and fashion boutiques are densely concentrated and where activewear functions not just as gym clothing but as daywear and a social identity signal.

This geographic context matters because patterns in Bondi or Double Bay differ meaningfully from outer suburban or regional Australia. Residents of the Eastern Suburbs are, on average, younger, more likely to be employed in professional or knowledge‑based occupations, more international in outlook, more likely to work flexible or hybrid hours, and more willing to pay a premium for lifestyle products that align with their values and aesthetics. These differences will be explicitly flagged when they diverge from broader Australian norms or US/UK benchmarks.

### 1.2 High‑confidence findings versus inferences

Because the target segment is specific - females aged roughly 18-45, engaged in Pilates and walking, with a strong interest in sustainable, aesthetically pleasing activewear and accessories, and living primarily in Sydney - fine‑grained syndicated reports may not exist for this exact niche. However, there is extensive research on related phenomena: Australian female fitness participation, activewear adoption, fashion and beauty spending, sustainable fashion attitudes, social media and influencer behaviours, and the growth of DTC brands.

To provide clarity around evidence strength, this report uses two conceptual categories:

High‑confidence findings are those consistent with multiple independent sources of Australian consumer data, broader demographic statistics, and stable global patterns that do not meaningfully differ in the Australian context. These include, for example, the concentration of high‑income young professionals in inner Sydney, the growth of Pilates and boutique fitness, the dominance of Instagram among young Australian women, and the documented gap between sustainability attitudes and actual purchase behaviour.

Inferences are reasoned extrapolations from those broader patterns to this specific micro‑segment, or more speculative estimates where exact quantitative figures are not publicly available. Examples include estimates of typical basket size for a niche DTC fitness accessories brand, or a finely detailed daily routine for the archetype consumer. These inferences are grounded in observed category dynamics and typical behaviours among similar segments, but they should be treated as hypotheses to be tested through direct market research.

Throughout the report, the distinction is signalled explicitly in the prose, using phrases such as “high confidence” or “it is likely that” and “a reasonable inference is.”

### 1.3 Category and product scope

The brand under consideration is a DTC fitness accessories company. Importantly, the term “fitness accessories” can be interpreted narrowly or broadly. For this report, a broad interpretation is adopted, encompassing:

Soft training accessories such as yoga and Pilates mats, resistance bands, ankle and wrist weights, Pilates rings, sliders, and foam rollers.

Wearable and styling accessories such as grip socks, headbands, scrunchies, caps or visors, gym towels, tote bags, cross‑body phone straps, drink bottles, and other items that accompany activewear.

Complementary items that are not apparel per se (leggings, sports bras) but are frequently co‑purchased and strongly coordinated with outfits, such as sneakers, slides, and small carry items for keys and phones.

The founder’s emphasis on “beautiful, sustainable activewear with matching shoes and accessories” suggests that the product strategy likely sits at the intersection of fashion, fitness, and lifestyle rather than performance‑only sports gear. This has implications for price positioning, design emphasis, and communication strategy, which will be explored in later sections.

## 2. Demographic Profile of the Target Segment

### 2.1 Age bands and life stage

The founder’s stated age range of 18-45 is broad, spanning late adolescence, tertiary education, early career, partnered and early parenting years, and in some cases established mid‑career professionals. In practice, the core DTC customer for a premium, design‑led fitness accessories brand is likely to be narrower, with the highest density of customers between 22 and 38 years of age. This narrower band aligns with several structural factors: higher independent purchasing power compared with teenagers, a strong cultural emphasis on wellness and appearance in early adulthood, and the higher adoption of boutique fitness and Pilates among young female professionals.

High‑confidence evidence across many markets, including Australia, shows that Pilates, yoga, and boutique fitness studios attract a disproportionate share of women aged 25-39, particularly in inner urban areas. This group tends to be willing to allocate substantial discretionary income to fitness memberships, athleisure fashion, and associated accessories. The upper edge of 40-45 is certainly present in boutique Pilates classes, particularly in affluent suburbs; however, their style preferences and brand choices may be slightly more conservative, and their digital engagement patterns, particularly on TikTok, are less intense than those of women in their twenties and early thirties. Therefore, a high‑confidence conclusion is that the “bullseye” customer is in her mid‑twenties to mid‑thirties, with a meaningful secondary audience in the late thirties to early forties and a smaller but still relevant tertiary audience of late teens and early twenties, often influenced by social media and peer emulation.

Life stage characteristics are as important as chronological age. A significant portion of this segment is likely to be child‑free, partnered or single, and renting or recently entering home ownership in inner‑city Sydney or coastal suburbs. Among those who do have children, they are more likely to have young children and to integrate fitness into early morning routines before school drop‑off or during childcare hours. The “young lady” descriptor used by the founder implicitly points toward a self‑perception of youthfulness that may extend slightly beyond the biological age range, especially in the Eastern Suburbs, where youthful aesthetics and active lifestyles are socially valorised.

### 2.2 Geographic concentration and urban context

Geographically, the segment is concentrated in metropolitan areas, with a strong weighting toward Sydney and within Sydney toward the Eastern Suburbs and near‑city neighbourhoods. Bondi, Bondi Beach, North Bondi, Tamarama, Bronte, Coogee, Double Bay, Paddington, Surry Hills, Darlinghurst, and similar locales provide the socio‑spatial context in which this consumer lives, socialises, and exercises. High‑confidence demographic patterns show that these suburbs have higher median incomes, higher rents and property prices, and a higher proportion of young adults compared with the national average.

In these areas, access to boutique Pilates studios, yoga studios, barre studios, and premium gyms is dense, and the coastal environment supports a strong culture of walking, running, and outdoor exercise along the beach and coastal walks. Activewear and athleisure are worn as everyday attire, not just workout clothing, particularly during the day and on weekends. Cafés, juice bars, and brunch spots are numerous, and there is a strong social scene around “walks and coffee,” “Pilates and brunch,” and “morning swim and smoothie” routines. In this context, the visual coherence and aesthetic appeal of activewear, sneakers, and accessories have high social visibility.

While the initial brand launch may focus on Sydney, there is likely to be a secondary concentration in other Australian capitals with similar urban‑coastal dynamics, particularly Melbourne (inner southeast, bayside, and inner north) and Brisbane (inner riverfront and coastal suburbs). However, compared with the US or UK, Australia’s population is more concentrated in a few major cities, and its DTC market is smaller in absolute terms. This creates both opportunities and constraints: the audience is more geographically clustered, which can support physical brand activations and localised marketing, but the overall scale is more limited, and expansion beyond Sydney will likely be necessary to sustain growth once the brand reaches maturity.

### 2.3 Income, occupation, and socioeconomic status

Affluent, design‑led fitness accessories with a sustainability positioning are, almost by definition, discretionary purchases. High‑confidence evidence from broader activewear and boutique fitness analyses indicates that boutique studio customers are significantly higher income than average, with many employed in professional, managerial, creative, or knowledge‑work roles. In Sydney’s Eastern Suburbs, occupations in professional services, finance, consulting, technology, marketing, media, design, healthcare, and education are prominent among women in the 22-38 age band, alongside students at university who are supported by family or part‑time work.

Reasonable inference suggests that many core segment members have individual pre‑tax incomes above the Australian median for women of the same age, with household incomes often well above median due to dual‑earning couples. While precise figures would require survey data, it is plausible that a large portion of the core cohort falls in the approximate individual income ranges that allow them to comfortably pay for multiple weekly boutique fitness classes, dine out regularly, take holidays, and invest in premium clothing and accessories. Some will be at higher income levels, especially in finance or professional services, while a meaningful minority may have lower incomes but prioritise these categories strongly, diverting spending from other areas.

Self‑employed or freelance work is also common, particularly among creative professionals, influencers, fitness instructors, and entrepreneurs. This leads to more flexible schedules, which align well with mid‑morning Pilates classes and daytime walks, and it can intensify social media use as both professional tool and leisure activity. For employees in more structured corporate roles, early morning (06:00-08:30) and after‑work (17:30-19:00) time slots are especially important for fitness, reinforcing the founder’s focus on “early morning Pilates classes and walks.”

### 2.4 Household composition and living arrangements

Household composition within this segment is diverse but shares some common patterns. A high‑confidence observation from urban demographic data is that inner‑city and beachside suburbs in Sydney have a high proportion of group households, couples without children, and single‑person households. Among the target segment, shared apartments or houses with roommates and couples in rented apartments or small townhouses are common. Shared living among young women in their twenties and early thirties often involves shared practices around fitness, meal prep, and social media, including following the same influencers, attending classes together, and sharing outfits or discount codes.

As the segment ages into the mid‑thirties and early forties, household composition tilts more toward couples and couples with young children. In these cases, especially where household income is higher, spending on activewear and accessories often remains strong, but the motivations may shift slightly toward convenience, quality, and durability. Women with young children frequently integrate pram walks into their routines, wear activewear throughout the day, and value accessories that are practical, easy to clean, and aesthetically pleasing enough to transition from playground to café. This is a slight Australia‑specific divergence from some US contexts where car‑centred suburbs reduce walking frequency; in Sydney’s coastal suburbs, walking culture is prominent and actively maintained through early motherhood.

### 2.5 Employment patterns and time use

Understanding employment patterns is critical because it shapes when and how the segment interacts with Instagram, TikTok, and email, and when they are most receptive to messaging about products and promotions. Among young professional women in Sydney, a hybrid work model is increasingly common, especially in knowledge‑based roles. This provides more flexibility to schedule mid‑morning or lunchtime Pilates classes on work‑from‑home days and contributes to a daily rhythm where short bursts of social media browsing and fitness‑related content consumption interleave with work tasks.

For those in more traditional 9‑to‑5 or 8‑to‑6 office roles, early morning slots are prized. It is typical for such consumers to attend a class at 06:00 or 06:30, shower, and then commute to the office or start work from home. Evening classes are also popular, but in the Eastern Suburbs context, there is a strong cultural premium placed on early morning rituals: sunrise walks along Bondi to Bronte, sunrise yoga on the beach, or first‑thing Pilates. This early‑morning orientation is somewhat stronger in Australia’s coastal cities than in dense European capitals, due to climate, daylight patterns, and the strong beach culture.

Part‑time workers, freelancers, and students often fill mid‑morning and lunchtime classes. Their social media usage can be more evenly spread across the day, and they are particularly likely to create and share their own Reels or TikToks featuring Pilates, outfits, and accessories. For the brand, this suggests that content should be optimised for early‑morning consumption (inspiration and motivation), late‑afternoon browsing (shopping and decision‑making), and sporadic mid‑day engagement.

## 3. Psychographic Profile: Values, Aspirations, and Daily Life

### 3.1 Core values and identity narratives

Psychographically, this segment is defined by an integrated value system that combines health, aesthetics, and ethics. High‑confidence patterns across similar urban wellness segments show that they strongly endorse values such as self‑care, balance, and holistic wellness, often extending beyond physical fitness to include mental health, mindfulness, and nutrition. Pilates, yoga, and walking align with these values by offering low‑impact, mindful movement that can be practised consistently without the burnout associated with high‑intensity training.

Aesthetics play a central role in identity. For this consumer, clothing and accessories are not just functional but expressive, allowing her to project a coherent visual narrative on Instagram and in real life. Feminine, colourful, and coordinated sets, along with matching shoes and accessories, form a key part of her “look.” She typically prefers styles that are polished yet relaxed, embracing pastel tones, soft neutrals, or curated pops of colour rather than only dark, aggressive, or performance‑athlete styles. This is especially pronounced in Bondi and Double Bay, where beach‑adjacent lifestyles lend themselves to lighter colours and “effortless” style, in contrast to some US urban centres where black and darker hues dominate.

Ethics and sustainability feature as important, though not always dominant, values. Many women in this segment describe themselves as environmentally conscious, care about reducing waste, and express concern about fast fashion. They value brands that use recycled or organic materials, minimise packaging, and maintain transparent supply chains. At the same time, they are not prepared to sacrifice fit, style, or comfort entirely for the sake of sustainability, and they are sensitive to perceived greenwashing. This tension between ideals and constraints is a central psychographic feature of the segment.

### 3.2 Aspirations and life goals

Aspirations in this segment often revolve around a blend of personal wellbeing, career development, relationship fulfilment, and a sense of “having it together.” Many aim to maintain a lean, toned physique, an organised lifestyle, and a curated social media presence, all while advancing in their careers or businesses. They are attracted to narratives of “balanced success” where one can wake up early, work out, drink a green smoothie, excel at work, maintain a vibrant social life, and still protect mental health.

Professional ambitions are typically significant, particularly among those in corporate or entrepreneurial roles. However, there is often a desire to avoid the burnout associated with older models of corporate success. Instead, they aspire to flexible work arrangements, remote or hybrid setups, side hustles, or careers in creative industries, wellness, or tech that offer autonomy and meaning. The adoption of Pilates and gentle fitness is sometimes framed not only as a path to physical health but also as an antidote to stress and a support for cognitive performance.

Relational and community aspirations are strong. The segment often values tight‑knit friendship groups, whether from school, university, or work, as well as community built around studios and gyms. Many aspire to be “that girl” in their friend group who is organised, stylish, and inspiring, or at least to align with such a persona. Social media amplifies these aspirations, as they follow influencers and peers who seem to embody them, particularly on Instagram and TikTok. This can be both motivating and anxiety‑inducing.

### 3.3 Anxieties, tensions, and pain points

Despite the glossy surface, this segment experiences significant underlying anxieties. One high‑confidence theme is the pressure to “do it all”: maintain a fit body, a successful career, a rich social life, and ethical shopping habits, all while staying within budget and managing mental health. Time scarcity is a chronic issue, especially for women balancing work, study, relationships, and possibly early motherhood. This makes convenience and frictionless experiences highly valued.

Body image concerns are widespread. Pilates, while gentler than high‑intensity workouts, is often associated with long, lean, “clean girl” aesthetics on social media, and many women internalise these ideals. They may worry about how they look in tight activewear, whether leggings show sweat, whether sports bras are supportive and flattering, or whether accessories like ankle weights and socks enhance or detract from their appearance. Apparel that cuts in at the wrong place, transparent leggings, or unflattering colours can be emotionally triggering. This is an area where design‑led brands can meaningfully reduce anxiety.

Financial tensions also play a role. Even among relatively high‑income women, the cost of living in Sydney, particularly rent and mortgages in the Eastern Suburbs, is substantial. Pilates studios and boutique fitness memberships are expensive, and premium activewear is similarly pricey. Consumers may feel guilty about spending too much on “non‑essential” items, yet simultaneously experience fear of missing out when peers and influencers continually showcase new outfits and accessories. Sustainability adds another layer of tension: they want to avoid fast fashion, yet cannot always justify high price premiums for sustainable alternatives.

### 3.4 Typical daily routines and touchpoints

A typical weekday routine for a core segment member in Bondi or Double Bay might look as follows, combining high‑confidence behavioural patterns with reasonable inference tailored to the founder’s description.

She wakes between 5:30 and 6:30 am, often checking her phone shortly after waking. Messages, Instagram, and occasionally TikTok are opened almost reflexively, with a quick scroll to catch up on overnight posts, stories, and Reels, including from local Pilates studios, fitness influencers, and friends. She might consult the weather, decide on her outfit, and choose between a walk along the coastal path, a Pilates or yoga class, or occasionally a gym session. In some cases, she pre‑books classes using studio apps, which may send reminders or promotional emails.

She dresses in a coordinated activewear set, selecting matching or complementary accessories such as sneakers, socks, cap, and a bag or tote. A water bottle, phone, keys, and possibly a small towel are organised. She may take a quick mirror selfie or outfit shot for Instagram Stories before heading out, especially if the outfit, accessories, or location feels particularly “post‑worthy.”

After exercise, she stops for coffee or a smoothie at a local café, often accompanied by a friend or partner. During this time, she may scroll Instagram again, save inspirational Reels, like posts from brands or studios, and possibly share content from her workout. If she has a flexible workday, she might continue social browsing on the commute or while at home, occasionally clicking through to brand profiles or websites from posts and ads.

The rest of the workday alternates between work tasks and brief social media breaks, particularly mid‑morning, lunchtime, and late afternoon. TikTok is more likely to be used during slightly longer breaks or in the evening. Email newsletters from brands are usually opened if they are short, visually pleasing, and offer clear value, such as early access to drops, styling tips, or special discounts. However, promotional emails risk being archived or deleted if overly frequent or generic.

In the evening, she may unwind with streaming content, additional social media browsing, or reading. TikTok usage often peaks in the later evening, where she might watch sequences of Pilates routines, “what I eat in a day,” outfit hauls, and lifestyle vlogs. These sessions are prime times for discovery of new products, but purchases may not occur immediately; instead, items are saved, screenshotted, or added to wishlists for later evaluation.

### 3.5 Fitness habits, goals, and identity

Within the fitness domain, this segment tends to favour consistency over extremes. High‑confidence patterns in boutique fitness show that women in this demographic typically attend Pilates, yoga, or similar low‑impact classes two to four times per week, supplementing with walks, occasional runs, or light strength training. The emphasis is on maintaining a toned, flexible body and good posture rather than achieving maximal strength, muscle mass, or competitive performance.

Pilates, in particular, is attractive for its focus on core strength, alignment, and grace, and it is often marketed through feminine, elegant imagery that matches the aesthetic preferences of this segment. Reformers and mat classes alike allow for visually appealing content capture, which further enhances the social media dimension. Many women identify as “Pilates girls” or “yoga girls,” and this identity intersects with other lifestyle markers such as particular cafés, fashion brands, skincare routines, and leisure activities.

Fitness goals are often articulated in terms of feeling good, having energy, and reducing stress, alongside physique goals such as “long, lean muscles” or a “flat stomach.” Recovery and rest are increasingly valorised, especially in the wake of broader conversations about burnout and nervous system regulation. Accessories such as foam rollers, massage balls, and stretching aids support this recovery orientation, while ankle weights, resistance bands, and small props support goal progression by adding challenge to home workouts and studio classes.

### 3.6 Media consumption, content preferences, and spending buckets

Media consumption for this segment is skewed towards digital and social channels. High‑confidence observations across many countries, including Australia, suggest that Instagram remains central for curated lifestyle content, while TikTok is the hub of short‑form entertainment, humour, and discovery. Streaming platforms like Netflix, Stan, Disney+, and others are significant for longer‑form viewing, often in the evening. Podcasts play a meaningful role on walks, commutes, and during light household chores, with popular genres including wellness, self‑development, business and entrepreneurship, and true crime.

Within Instagram, this segment engages heavily with Stories, Reels, and the grid. Stories are used to maintain ambient awareness of friends, influencers, and brands; Reels serve as discovery and inspiration; and the grid is used for more polished self‑presentation. Within TikTok, “For You” page content is dominated by algorithmically surfaced videos rather than accounts they explicitly follow, though they do follow favourite creators. Pilates, fitness, fashion, recipes, home organisation, and “day in the life” vlogs are common categories.

In terms of spending priorities, this segment allocates substantial discretionary income to a set of interrelated categories: rent or mortgage in an attractive neighbourhood, boutique fitness memberships or class packs, dining out and coffee, travel and holidays, fashion and beauty, and digital subscriptions. Activewear and accessories fall at the intersection of fashion and fitness, competing for budget with both new dresses and additional classes. However, because activewear can be worn daily and is visible in both fitness and social contexts, many women justify higher spending in this category by framing it as an investment in confidence, motivation, and social presentation.

## 4. Category Behaviour: Products, Brands, and Price Points

### 4.1 Existing purchase behaviour in fitness apparel and accessories

The target segment is already highly active in purchasing fitness apparel and accessories. High‑confidence evidence from the growth of athleisure in Australia indicates that many women maintain multiple sets of activewear, often from a mix of premium and mid‑range brands. Among Sydney’s Eastern Suburbs, it is common to see women wearing brands associated with both global and local labels, and to treat activewear as a legitimate outfit for social settings.

In practice, this means that a typical consumer within the segment may own several pairs of leggings, multiple sports bras, various tops, and several pairs of sneakers specifically for workouts, as well as accessories like mats, resistance bands, foam rollers, and grip socks. She may also own specialised Pilates socks with grips, ankle weights for home workouts, and a specific bag or tote that is designated as her “gym bag,” even if it also functions as a daily carry.

Many of these products are bought over time rather than in single large hauls. For example, she might invest in a premium set from a well‑known brand every few months, supplemented by more affordable or trend‑driven items from mid‑tier brands or fast fashion retailers. Accessories can be more frequently replaced or added because they are lower unit‑cost items, although premium accessories like high‑quality mats or ankle weights can be relatively expensive.

### 4.2 Price bands for key product types (AUD)

In the Australian context, price points for fitness apparel and accessories fall into several tiers. The following descriptions are approximate and based on typical market positioning; they are intended as a high‑level guide rather than precise figures.

Premium leggings and sports bras from established activewear brands generally fall into the moderate‑to‑premium price band. Sets from well‑known global or local brands in Australia commonly occupy this range, with some designs higher depending on fabric technology or limited edition collaborations. Mid‑range activewear from large retail chains and online fashion retailers is typically cheaper, with frequent promotions that lower the effective price. Budget options from discount department stores, fast fashion chains, or no‑name brands can be significantly cheaper again, but they often sacrifice quality, fit, and aesthetics, and are less appealing for a segment that values design and sustainability.

Fitness accessories mirror this stratification. Premium yoga and Pilates mats from design‑driven and eco‑conscious brands can be relatively costly but are marketed as durable, non‑slip, and aesthetically pleasing, sometimes with unique prints or colours. Mid‑range mats and accessories sold through sporting goods chains and mainstream retailers are more affordable and widely available. At the budget end, mass‑market retailers and discount chains sell functional but basic mats, bands, and weights at very low prices, often in limited colour options and with minimal attention to design coherence across items.

Specialised accessories such as aesthetically designed ankle weights and hand weights, particularly those made to be worn on walks or in Pilates classes and marketed via Instagram, often command premium pricing relative to their functional equivalents in mainstream sporting goods stores. Similarly, stylish grip socks, visors, caps, gym towels, and bags can be priced higher than generic versions due to design value, branding, and alignment with fashion trends.

Overall, for a core consumer in the described segment, spending between approximately one to several hundred dollars in a single purchase on a combination of apparel and accessories is not unusual, particularly when triggered by a new drop, a payday, or a perceived wardrobe gap. For accessories only, a typical basket might include one or two higher‑priced items and one or two lower‑priced ones, resulting in a basket around the low‑ to mid‑hundreds for a premium DTC brand. These figures are inferences, but they align with observed behaviours in similar categories such as beauty and fashion among the same demographic.

## Part 1: The lay of the land in 2026

### The five channels every brand operates on

Modern marketing runs across five channels. Most brands underweight at least two of them, which is usually why they're stuck.

1. **Paid media.** You pay a platform (Meta, TikTok, Google, YouTube) to put your message in front of strangers. Fast, scalable, expensive, and increasingly noisy. The "performance marketing" you hear about.
2. **Organic social.** You post for free on platforms you don't own (Instagram, TikTok, Facebook, YouTube). The algorithm decides who sees it. Cheap, slow to compound, but the most authentic touchpoint when it works.
3. **Creator and influencer.** Other people post about you. Either they're paid (sponsored), gifted product (UGC and seeding), or genuinely fans (advocacy). The fastest-growing channel for DTC in 2026.
4. **Owned media.** Channels you fully control. Email, SMS, your blog, your podcast, your customer list. Slower to build but no platform can take it away from you.
5. **PR, partnerships, and earned media.** Press coverage, collaborations with other brands, podcast features. Not your daily driver, but a leverage point for big moments.

A real brand needs at least three of these working at once. Sportif will need paid (Part 2), organic (Part 3), email (Part 4), and creator (Part 5) all running by launch.

### The funnel concept

The funnel is just the journey from "never heard of you" to "loyal customer." Every channel and every piece of creative work fits somewhere on it.

| Stage | What it means | What works here |
|---|---|---|
| **Awareness** | "There's a brand called Sportif." | Top-of-feed video ads, creator content, viral organic, PR |
| **Consideration** | "I'm curious about Sportif." | Product demos, founder story, comparison reviews, retargeting ads |
| **Conversion** | "I'm buying from Sportif." | Sale ads, abandoned-cart email, urgency offers, free shipping |
| **Retention** | "I'm buying again from Sportif." | Post-purchase email, loyalty program, new-arrival drops |
| **Advocacy** | "I'm telling friends about Sportif." | Referral programs, UGC contests, community |

The mistake most new brands make: they spend 90% of their budget at the conversion stage, then can't understand why nothing's converting. The funnel only converts if there's enough volume at the top. You feed the top, the bottom takes care of itself.

### What changed between 2021 and 2026

The marketing playbook from 2021 is broken in five specific ways. Knowing why each broke helps you avoid the recommendations from people still living in that world.

1. **iOS 14 privacy changes (2021).** Apple let users opt out of cross-app tracking. Before, Meta could follow a user from Instagram to your website to your checkout and tell its algorithm exactly who converts. After, Meta lost most of that visibility. Result: Meta ads became less precise, more expensive per real customer, and harder to attribute.
2. **CPM inflation.** Cost to reach 1,000 people on Meta roughly doubled between 2021 and 2024. Same in Australia, with Australian Meta CPMs running around $9.80 AUD in 2026, up 12% year on year. Translation: the ads that worked at $34 CPM stop working at $57 CPM because the unit economics flip.
3. **Creator economy maturation.** Instagram and TikTok now actively promote creator-style content over polished brand content. A real person holding the product on camera beats a studio shot in almost every test. Creator partnerships went from "nice to have" to the primary acquisition engine for fitness DTC.
4. **AI creative tools went mainstream.** What used to take a production crew (HeyGen avatars, ChatGPT image gen, Seadance video, HyperFrames code-rendered video) is now a solo workflow. Brands that integrate AI into their creative cycle ship 5 to 10x more variants than competitors stuck on traditional production. (This is why Ochoproductions exists.)
5. **Restricted Health and Wellness rules tightened.** Meta and TikTok both tightened enforcement on fitness/wellness ads in 2026. Detail covered in Part 2, but the headline: anything claiming a specific health outcome ("burns X calories," "boosts performance Y%") gets dropped into a restricted bucket where your ads optimize worse. Solution: lead with aesthetic and function, not outcomes.

### The "no single channel is enough" reality

The single biggest learning from the DTC graveyard report (50 documented brand failures) is this: every dead brand was over-indexed on one channel. Usually Meta ads. The ones that survived built three or four channels in parallel so when one platform changed the rules, the others held the line.

Your job at Sportif is not to find "the" channel. Your job is to build a small portfolio of channels that compound on each other. Organic content makes creator collabs cheaper. Creator content makes paid ads convert better. Paid ads grow the email list. The email list fuels retention without needing more paid spend.

### The flywheel framing

If "funnel" describes one customer's journey, "flywheel" describes how a brand grows over time. The flywheel for a fitness DTC brand in 2026 looks like this:

```
Creator content → audience awareness → website visits → email signups
       ↑                                                    ↓
   loyalty &                                            first purchase
   advocacy ←─── retention emails ←─── happy customers ←──┘
```

The flywheel spins faster the more friction you remove between steps. Most brands have a great front door (creator content) and a bad back door (no email flows, no loyalty, no retention). You build a brand by smoothing the whole loop, not by throwing more money at the front door.

## Part 2: Paid ads, platform by platform

### How an ad auction actually works

Every paid platform (Meta, TikTok, Google) uses some version of the same system. It's an auction, but not for impressions in the way you'd guess.

When you set up an ad, you tell the platform three things:
1. **Who you want to reach.** Audience targeting.
2. **What you want them to do.** Optimization objective (view, click, buy, sign up).
3. **How much you're willing to spend.** Daily or lifetime budget plus a bid strategy.

When a user opens the app, the platform runs an instant auction in the background. Every advertiser targeting that user with that objective is bidding for that single impression. The winner isn't necessarily the highest bidder. It's the highest **expected value**, calculated as:

`(your bid) x (predicted click-through rate) x (predicted conversion rate)`

This is the most important thing to understand about paid ads: **the platform rewards ads it predicts will convert, not ads with the highest bid.** Better creative beats higher spend. A $5 CPM ad with a 3% click rate will beat a $15 CPM ad with a 0.3% click rate every single time.

This also explains why "the algorithm" feels mysterious. The platform is constantly learning who clicks and who buys, then routing future impressions toward people who behave similarly. Your job is to feed it clear signals and good creative.

### Meta (Facebook and Instagram)

#### What it is

Facebook and Instagram share one ad system, called Meta Ads. You build campaigns in one place (Meta Ads Manager or Meta Business Suite) and Meta decides which placements (Facebook feed, IG feed, Stories, Reels, Marketplace, Audience Network) to show them on, optimizing for results.

#### Account structure (the three-layer hierarchy)

Every Meta ad campaign has three layers:

1. **Campaign** = sets the objective (e.g., "drive website purchases" or "build awareness").
2. **Ad set** = sets the audience, placement, budget, and bid.
3. **Ad** = the actual creative the user sees.

One campaign can have multiple ad sets. One ad set can have multiple ads. This matters because Meta optimizes within each layer:
- Meta optimizes across **ads** within an ad set automatically (showing the best-performing creative more).
- Meta does NOT optimize across **ad sets** automatically (it'll spend even budget on poor ones unless you intervene or use Advantage Campaign Budget).
- You're responsible for what objective and audience structure to use.

#### Targeting in 2026

iOS 14 ate a lot of the precise targeting Meta used to offer. What's left:

- **Detailed targeting.** Interest categories (e.g., "Pilates," "Yoga," "Fitness and wellness"). Less precise than 2020 but still useful.
- **Custom audiences.** Lists you upload (email list, customer file, website visitors via Meta Pixel). The most valuable category Meta still offers.
- **Lookalike audiences.** Meta finds strangers who behave like your custom audience. Used to be the engine of DTC. Now degraded for any brand classified as Restricted Health and Wellness.
- **Advantage+ Audience.** Meta's "let us figure it out" mode. Increasingly the recommended default for cold acquisition.

For Sportif: don't bet the launch on lookalikes. Build a custom audience of website visitors and email signups via the Meta Pixel from day one, but plan to use Advantage+ Audience for cold acquisition and interest-targeting for tighter campaigns.

#### Creative formats that work

For fitness accessories specifically:
- **Reels and Stories vertical video (9:16 ratio), 6 to 15 seconds.** The dominant format. Lead with the product in the first 1.5 seconds. Native, hand-held, creator-led beats polished brand video roughly 3:1 in click-through rate.
- **Static images for retargeting.** Cheap to produce, useful for warm audiences who already know the brand.
- **Carousels for product details.** Multiple swipeable images, good for showing colour variants or product features.
- **UGC compilations.** Three to five real customer clips edited together. Often the best-performing format for accessories.

What doesn't work: heavily produced brand films, talking-head founder ads longer than 30 seconds, ads with text-heavy first frames.

These come from public benchmarks across all industries. Health and Fitness typically runs at or below average:

| Average CPC (cost per click) | $1.47 AUD | ~£0.90 | ~$2.10 USD |
| Instagram CPC | $1.83 to $3.35 AUD | higher than FB | higher than FB |
| Facebook CPC | $1.06 to $1.72 AUD | similar split | similar split |

#### Realistic budget bands

Below the minimum, Meta's algorithm doesn't get enough data to learn properly. These are the rough thresholds.

- **Lean ($300 to $1,000 AUD/month).** Retargeting only. Don't try cold acquisition at this budget on Meta. Use it to convert website visitors and email signups.
- **Mid ($2,000 to $5,000 AUD/month).** Now you can start cold acquisition. Expect 30 to 60 days of "learning phase" before performance stabilises.
- **Scale ($10,000+ AUD/month).** You're in real performance-marketing territory. You need a dedicated media buyer or Triple Whale or Northbeam to track properly.

For Sportif's launch, the realistic answer is "lean to mid" depending on Lucy's runway. Meta is amplification, not acquisition, in the first six months.

#### The 2026 Restricted Health and Wellness trap

Recap from the SWOT: Meta classifies any fitness or wellness brand that makes specific health-outcome claims (e.g., "improves cardiovascular performance," "burns X calories," "reduces soreness by Y%") into a Restricted Health and Wellness bucket. Once in:
- Meta restricts the brand from optimising on lower-funnel conversion events (purchases, bookings). You're stuck optimising on weaker events (clicks, views).
- Meta auto-flags any audience or conversion event whose name implies sensitive traits (body image, weight, fitness goals).

**The lever Sportif controls:** write product copy in aesthetic, lifestyle, and function language ("these look beautiful and they make every workout count"), not outcome-claim language. You stay outside the restricted bucket. Same product, different framing, very different ad performance.

### TikTok

#### What it is

TikTok has its own ad platform (TikTok Ads Manager). Same auction logic as Meta but with a meaningfully different creative culture. TikTok was the first platform to make "ads that look like organic posts" the dominant format. If your TikTok ad looks like an ad, it dies.

#### Account structure

Same three-layer hierarchy as Meta: Campaign, Ad Group, Ad. Same logic about objectives and budgets.

#### Targeting

TikTok's targeting is less granular than Meta's but the algorithm is stronger at audience expansion. You give it a starting audience, and it learns who else to show your ad to with relatively little hand-holding. The platform leans hard on creative signal: TikTok actively rewards ads with high watch time, comments, and shares.

#### Creative formats that work

- **Spark Ads.** TikTok's defining ad format. You take an organic post (yours or a creator's, with permission) and promote it. It looks and feels native because it IS native. Spark Ads consistently outperform "branded" videos by a wide margin and are usually the right default for fitness accessories.
- **In-Feed Ads.** Standard 9:16 video ads in the FYP. Should still feel raw and creator-style, not polished.
- **TopView and Brand Takeover.** Premium placements (first ad after opening the app). Expensive, mostly used for big-brand awareness pushes. Skip for Sportif's stage.
- **Branded Hashtag Challenges.** Users participate in a challenge using your hashtag. Expensive minimum spend (usually $50K+ USD). Skip.

#### TikTok content rules that matter for fitness

- **No before-and-after transformation imagery in paid ads.** Banned across the board. No weight-loss reveals, no muscle-development cuts, no "results" visuals. This is unavoidable on TikTok paid regardless of your claim language.
- **No medical or health-outcome implications.** Same logic as Meta but enforced harder on TikTok.
- **Music rights matter.** Commercial Music Library only for paid ads. Trending sounds you see on organic FYP aren't all usable on paid.

#### Australian TikTok benchmarks (2026)

| Metric | Australia |
|---|---|
| CPM (broad targeting) | $4 to $10 AUD per 1,000 impressions |
| CPC | $0.40 to $1.00 AUD |
| Health and Fitness vertical CPM | ~$6.50 AUD (most affordable vertical) |
| Sydney audience CPM premium | +20 to 50% in peaks |

TikTok runs about 30% cheaper than Meta in Australia, which is the same arbitrage as global trends but the gap is closing. Source: [MPD Australia TikTok Cost Guide](https://mediaplusdigital.co/au/tiktok-advertising-cost/).

#### Realistic budget bands for Sportif scale

- **Lean ($500 to $1,500 AUD/month).** TikTok minimum daily spend at campaign level is $50 AUD, ad-group level $20 AUD. Below ~$500/month total you don't get enough data.
- **Mid ($2,000 to $5,000 AUD/month).** Real cold acquisition possible. Spark Ads from creator partners work best here.
- **Scale ($10,000+ AUD/month).** TikTok's auction is less mature than Meta's, so scaling is choppier. Expect more performance variance.

### Google Ads (briefly)

Google Search ads catch demand that already exists. Someone types "best wrist weights Pilates" into Google, your ad shows. High intent, high cost, narrow ceiling.

For fitness accessories pre-launch, Google Search is mostly not your channel. You're trying to build awareness for a new brand. Nobody is searching for Sportif yet. Once the brand is known, branded search (people Googling "Sportif weights") is cheap and important to capture. Plan to turn this on around launch with a small monthly budget ($300 to $500 AUD), targeting only brand-name keywords.

Google Shopping (the product carousel above Search results) is useful once Sportif has a Google Merchant Center feed connected to Shopify. Worth turning on at launch.

### YouTube ads (briefly)

YouTube ads run through Google Ads. Two formats matter:
- **In-stream skippable ads** (the 5-second-then-skip ads before YouTube videos).
- **YouTube Shorts ads** (vertical short-form, similar feel to TikTok/Reels).

YouTube has higher production value expectations and longer attention than TikTok. For Sportif pre-launch, defer YouTube paid until you have one or two flagship videos worth amplifying.

### What to do with all this for Sportif

You don't need to be an expert in all four platforms. You need to know enough to pick the right two for launch (Meta and TikTok), avoid the Restricted Health and Wellness trap, and write creative that the platforms actually reward (raw, creator-style, vertical, 6 to 15 seconds, product-led in the first frame).

## Part 3: Organic content, platform by platform

Paid ads cost money. Organic content takes time but compounds. The brands that win in 2026 do both, with organic feeding paid (better creative, audience signals) and paid feeding organic (more eyes, more followers).

### How organic algorithms decide what to show

Every platform's algorithm asks the same questions in different orders:

1. **Is this content engaging?** Watch time, completion rate, replays, likes, comments, shares, saves.
2. **Is this content recent and fresh?** Most platforms decay older posts hard.
3. **Is this content from someone the viewer engages with?** Follow-graph signals.
4. **Is this content matching what this viewer recently watched?** Topic clustering.
5. **Is this safe and policy-compliant?** Anything flagged gets suppressed quietly.

The two signals that matter most for organic reach: **completion rate** (did people watch to the end) and **shares** (did people send it to a friend or save it for later). Optimize for those two and most algorithms surface your content.

### Instagram

#### The three formats and what each is for

- **Reels.** Short vertical video. Where the algorithm currently spends most of its discovery effort. By far the highest reach potential for accounts under 10K followers. This is where you build audience.
- **Feed posts.** Static images and carousels. Lower reach but higher save rate. Use for product education, brand identity, and content that deserves to be revisited.
- **Stories.** 24-hour ephemeral posts. Low discovery but high engagement from your existing followers. Use for behind-the-scenes, polls, link-outs, and product launches.

You need all three, but Reels does the heavy lifting for growth.

#### What works for fitness accessories on Instagram

- **Reels showing real use of the product.** A Pilates instructor wearing the wrist weights, no studio set, casual phone footage. Authenticity beats production.
- **Hooks in the first 1.5 seconds.** Either visual (interesting motion, product reveal) or text overlay that creates curiosity ("Stop doing this with your wrist weights").
- **Trending audio.** Use the audio library to pick songs trending in the last 7 days. Instagram boosts trending audio.
- **9:16 with safe zones.** Keep important visuals away from the bottom 20% (covered by UI) and top 15%.
- **Captions matter for SEO.** Instagram's search now indexes captions. Use the keywords your buyers would type.

#### Posting cadence

- 4 to 5 Reels per week minimum for growth-phase brands.
- 2 to 3 Feed posts per week.
- 5 to 10 Story posts per day (this number sounds high; chunk it across the day).

#### The "content pillars" concept

Pick 3 to 5 themes your content rotates through, so followers learn what to expect. For Sportif, examples might be:
1. Product in motion (Reels of someone using the weights in real workouts).
2. Founder voice (Lucy POV, behind-the-scenes building the brand).
3. Education (form tips, workout style explainers).
4. Community (UGC reshares, customer features once launched).
5. Aesthetic (slower, beautiful product shots that reinforce the brand).

### TikTok

#### How the FYP (For You Page) works

TikTok's algorithm is the most aggressive at testing new content. Every video you post gets a small initial push (usually a few hundred views). If engagement signals (completion rate, shares, comments) are strong, TikTok pushes the next batch (thousands of views). If those signals hold, it keeps pushing. This is why "going viral" feels random on TikTok: a single great video can hit a million views from a small account, while ten mediocre videos die at 200 views each.

#### What works for fitness accessories on TikTok

- **Hooks in the first 0.8 seconds.** TikTok is faster than Instagram. The first frame must stop the scroll.
- **Conversational, not produced.** Talking direct to camera, holding the product, demoing in real conditions. Studio-quality footage actively hurts.
- **Pattern interrupts.** Sudden cuts, captions appearing, on-screen text that creates curiosity. The viewer's thumb is one centimeter from scrolling. Every second has to earn attention.
- **Trending sounds.** TikTok's algorithm openly favours videos using trending audio in the last 3 to 7 days. Check the TikTok Creative Center for current trending sounds.
- **Show, don't tell.** "Watch how this changes a Pilates flow" beats "These weights enhance your workout."

#### Posting cadence

3 to 5 TikToks per week minimum. More is better up to about 10 per week before quality drops.

### Facebook (organic)

The unsexy truth: Facebook organic reach for brand Pages in 2026 is brutal. Most posts reach less than 5% of your followers. Treat the Facebook Page as a credibility signal (people will check it when researching the brand) and a hub for Facebook Groups, but not as a daily content channel.

**Facebook Groups** are different. They have higher organic reach and engagement than Pages, but they belong to the community, not the brand. Sportif could create a "Sportif Community" Group at launch, but should focus on adding value (Pilates tips, customer Q&As, community challenges) rather than promotion.

### YouTube (organic)

#### Two YouTube products that matter

- **YouTube Shorts.** Vertical short-form, same format as TikTok/Reels. Less culturally dominant than TikTok but the algorithm is generous to new accounts.
- **YouTube long-form.** 8 to 20 minute videos. Massive compounding effect over years (a great video keeps getting recommended for months or years), but very slow to start.

For Sportif's stage, focus on Shorts (re-purpose TikTok content with minor edits). Defer long-form until you have something that justifies the production cost, like a founder story documentary or an in-depth product manufacturing video.

### The repurposing system

Don't make different content for each platform. Make one piece of content, then adapt it.

1. Shoot vertical (9:16) by default.
2. Post first to TikTok (where rough authenticity is rewarded most).
3. Repost to Instagram Reels (same video, maybe tighter edit).
4. Re-cut to YouTube Shorts (add a TikTok-style hook overlay).
5. Pull stills for Instagram Feed or carousel.
6. Use clips for Stories with sticker call-to-action.

One filming session can produce 2 weeks of content this way. This is the Ochoproductions advantage: the AI creative stack accelerates the editing and repurposing step from hours to minutes.

## Part 4: Email marketing

### Why email is the most underrated channel

After all the platform changes since 2020 (iOS 14, cookie deprecation, CPM inflation), email has quietly become the most reliable channel a DTC brand owns. Reasons:

1. **You own the list.** No platform can ban you, throttle reach, or change targeting rules. Your list is yours forever.
2. **Direct delivery.** Email lands in an inbox. No algorithm decides who sees it.
3. **High ROI.** Industry benchmark across DTC is $36 to $40 in revenue per $1 of email spend. For ecommerce-focused tools like Klaviyo, the multiplier is even higher.
4. **Works at any scale.** A list of 500 engaged subscribers is genuinely valuable. A list of 50,000 can carry most of a brand's revenue.

Most new DTC brands underinvest in email for the first 12 months and regret it. The earliest you can start collecting emails (well before launch), the better.

### The core flows every ecommerce brand needs

A "flow" is an automated email sequence triggered by an event. You build them once, they run forever. The five that matter most:

1. **Welcome flow.** Triggered when someone signs up. Three to five emails over the first week, introducing the brand, sharing the founder story, and inviting a first purchase with a small incentive. Industry benchmark: welcome flows generate 4 to 8x the revenue of regular campaigns.
2. **Abandoned cart flow.** Triggered when someone adds to cart but doesn't check out. Two to three emails over 24 to 72 hours. Often the highest-revenue flow in the whole account.
3. **Browse abandonment flow.** Triggered when someone views a product but doesn't add to cart. Lower urgency than abandoned cart, but high-volume.
4. **Post-purchase flow.** Triggered after first purchase. Three to four emails over 30 days: thank you, how to use the product, ask for review, suggest add-on purchase.
5. **Win-back flow.** Triggered when a customer hasn't purchased in 60 to 90 days. One to three emails offering a reason to return.

In addition to flows, brands send **campaigns** (one-off broadcasts to the whole list or a segment). Examples: new product launches, sales, content drops, founder updates.

For ecommerce, the right answer in 2026 is almost always **Klaviyo**. It integrates deeply with Shopify, has the strongest pre-built flow templates for DTC, and generates roughly 3.8x the revenue per subscriber compared to Mailchimp.

### Deliverability basics

If your emails land in spam, none of this matters. Three things to know:

1. **Authentication.** Set up SPF, DKIM, and DMARC records on Sportif's domain. Klaviyo will walk you through this. Without it, deliverability tanks.
2. **Engagement signals matter.** Gmail and Outlook watch who opens, clicks, and replies. A list of 100 highly engaged subscribers is more valuable than a list of 10,000 ignored ones. Don't add anyone to the list who didn't ask to be there.
3. **Warm up gradually.** A brand-new domain sending 10,000 emails on day one will get spam-flagged. Send small at first, grow volume over weeks.

### List building from social

Every Instagram bio, every TikTok bio, every YouTube description should drive to a single landing page that captures email. The offer matters: "Get 15% off your first order" works but is generic. "Join the founding 1,000 and get early access plus a free colour swap" works better for a pre-launch brand because it's exclusive.

For Sportif specifically, pre-launch waitlist building is one of the three strategic priorities from the SWOT. Target 1,000+ emails before September with a real incentive (early access, founding-customer discount, exclusive colourway).

## Part 5: Creator and influencer marketing

### Why this is the new acquisition engine for fitness DTC

Three things converged to make creators the dominant channel for fitness accessories in 2026:

1. **Platforms reward creator content over brand content.** Instagram and TikTok both algorithmically boost native, creator-style content. A brand video gets less reach than a creator video of the same product.
2. **Paid ad costs went up while creator content stayed cheap (per impression).** A creator with 50K engaged followers can deliver more relevant impressions than a $5,000 paid ad campaign, often at a fraction of the cost.
3. **Trust collapsed for brands and stayed high for individuals.** Buyers trust real people demonstrating a product more than any polished campaign. ROI on creator content can run as high as $5.78 for every $1 spent in fitness/health specifically. Source: [Sprout Social Australia fitness creator data](https://sproutsocial.com/insights/fitness-influencers-australia/).

### Terminology decoded

Four overlapping terms get confused. Here's how to think about them:

- **UGC (User-Generated Content).** Content made by a customer or paid creator that looks like it was made by a regular person. Often gifted product in exchange. Rights: the brand can usually use the content in paid ads.
- **Influencer marketing.** Paid partnership with a creator who has audience and influence. They post about your product on their feed for a fee, with full disclosure (#ad). The creator's followers see it.
- **Ambassador.** Long-term creator partnership, often with a small monthly retainer plus product, plus commission on sales. Deeper relationship than a one-off post.
- **Creator partnership.** Catch-all term used loosely. Often means a hybrid: paid post plus UGC rights plus ambassador status over time.

In practice: most Sportif relationships will be a mix. Gift product, ask for a Reel, agree on usage rights in advance, pay for top-tier collaborators, keep the best 4 to 6 on a small monthly retainer.

### The four tiers (with Australian follower bands and rough rates)

| Tier | Followers | Australian fitness rate per post (rough) | Best for |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Nano** | 1K to 10K | Product gifting only, or $50 to $200 AUD | Authenticity, high engagement, low cost. Great for early Sportif. |
| **Micro** | 10K to 100K | $200 to $2,000 AUD | The sweet spot for most DTC. Real reach, still authentic. |
| **Mid** | 100K to 500K | $2,000 to $10,000 AUD | Real awareness lifts. Need vetting to make sure engagement is genuine. |
| **Mega** | 500K+ | $10,000 to $100,000+ AUD | Big-brand budgets. Usually not the right fit for a launch like Sportif. |

A common mistake: paying for one mega creator instead of ten micros. The ten micros almost always outperform on conversion because their audiences are smaller, more engaged, and feel like a real recommendation. Save mega creator spend for once the brand is established.

### How to find creators

Three workflows, in order of cost:

1. **Manual on platform (free, slowest).** Search hashtags (#pilatesaustralia, #pilatesgirlsau, #pilatessydney), open profiles, build a spreadsheet. Slow but you get to vet vibe and authenticity in person.
2. **Free creator databases.** [Modash](https://www.modash.io/) and [Clickanalytic](https://www.clickanalytic.com/) have free Australian fitness rankings. Good for shortlisting.
3. **Paid platforms ($100 to $500/mo).** Tools like Modash Pro, GRIN, or Upfluence let you filter by follower count, location, engagement rate, and category. Worth it once you're contacting 50+ creators per month.

### How to brief a creator

Two extremes to avoid: too tight (creators hate being told what to say, audience can smell scripted content) or too loose (the post misses your brand point entirely). The right middle:

1. **Brand 30-second context.** Who Sportif is, the one-line positioning, the tone.
2. **One product, one core message.** Don't ask them to cover three things.
3. **Required mentions** (limit to 2 to 3): product name, key feature or two, call to action (link in bio, code).
4. **Hard rules** (limit to 5): what they cannot say, claim, or imply (key for Sportif: no health-outcome claims).
5. **What you'll provide.** Product, usage rights agreement, optional script suggestions (label as optional).
6. **Deliverables and timeline.** What posts, when, where, in what format.
7. **Compensation and usage.** Cash, gifted, both. How long the brand can re-use the content. Whether the brand can boost it as Spark Ads or whitelist as Meta ads.

### How to compensate

| Compensation type | When to use |
|---|---|
| **Gifted only** | Nano and micro tiers, your first 6 creators. Frame as "we'd love your honest feedback." Don't expect every gifted creator to post; budget for 40 to 60% post rate. |
| **Cash + gifted** | Mid-tier creators or proven micros. Per-post fee plus product. |
| **Monthly retainer + commission** | Ambassadors. Smaller monthly fee ($300 to $1,000 AUD) plus a percent of sales via their unique code or link. |
| **Hybrid usage rights** | Almost always negotiate the right to re-use the content as Spark Ads on TikTok and as Meta whitelisted ads. This unlocks the creator's content as paid creative. |

## Part 6: The metrics and tools stack

### The acronyms, defined once

| Acronym | Stands for | Means | When you care |
|---|---|---|---|
| **CPM** | Cost Per Mille | Cost per 1,000 ad impressions | Reach and awareness campaigns |
| **CPC** | Cost Per Click | Cost per ad click | Click-driving campaigns |
| **CTR** | Click-Through Rate | Clicks ÷ impressions, as a % | Creative quality signal |
| **CVR** | Conversion Rate | Conversions ÷ clicks, as a % | Landing page and offer quality signal |
| **CPA** | Cost Per Acquisition | Cost per conversion (signup, purchase) | Acquisition campaigns |
| **ROAS** | Return On Ad Spend | Revenue ÷ ad spend, as a multiple (3x means $3 of revenue per $1 spent) | The single most reported paid-ad metric |
| **AOV** | Average Order Value | Average $ value per order | Revenue planning |
| **LTV** | Lifetime Value | Total $ a customer spends over their relationship with the brand | The real number behind whether paid ads are profitable |
| **CAC** | Customer Acquisition Cost | Cost to acquire one paying customer | The other side of the LTV equation |
| **LTV:CAC** | Ratio | LTV ÷ CAC. Healthy DTC target: 3:1 or higher | Brand sustainability |
| **MER** | Marketing Efficiency Ratio | Total revenue ÷ total marketing spend (all channels). | The "are we profitable overall" metric |
| **ROI** | Return on Investment | Generic profit measure. | Used loosely. Less precise than ROAS or MER. |
| **MOF / TOF / BOF** | Middle / Top / Bottom of Funnel | Where in the funnel a campaign sits | Campaign segmentation |

### The basic dashboard you need

For Sportif at launch stage, the metrics that matter weekly are:

1. **Email list size and weekly signups.** Pre-launch, this is the leading indicator of demand.
2. **Organic followers per platform and weekly growth.** Trend, not absolute number.
3. **Top three organic posts by reach and saves.** These are the creative signals to lean into.
4. **Paid ad spend, ROAS, and CAC per platform.** Once paid is live.
5. **Website sessions and conversion rate.** From Shopify dashboard or Google Analytics 4 (GA4).
6. **Email open rate, click rate, and revenue per send.** From Klaviyo.

You don't need a fancy tool to track these in the first six months. A weekly Google Sheet with manual numbers works fine.

### Attribution: the broken state of the art

Since iOS 14, attribution is genuinely broken. The number Meta reports in Ads Manager rarely matches the number Shopify reports for the same campaign. Two reasons:

1. **Platform self-reporting bias.** Meta wants to take credit for every sale it touches, even if the buyer would have bought anyway.
2. **Click-window mismatches.** Meta tracks sales within 7 days of an ad click. Shopify tracks sales whenever they happen. The two systems disagree often.

What brands do about it:

- **Look at MER instead of ROAS.** MER = total revenue ÷ total marketing spend. If you spent $10K total and revenue went from $40K to $60K, your MER is 2x and you can be confident the spend lifted the business, regardless of which platform "claimed" the sale.
- **Use post-purchase surveys.** Ask every customer at checkout: "How did you hear about us?" Two months of data shows you what's actually driving sales.
- **Use third-party attribution tools** like Triple Whale ($300 to $1,500 USD/month) or Northbeam once spend exceeds $20K/month. Below that, post-purchase surveys + MER are enough.

### Tools stack for Sportif at launch

Suggested minimum viable stack:

| Layer | Tool | Cost (AUD/month approx.) |
|---|---|---|
| Storefront | Shopify | $50 to $135 |
| Email | Klaviyo | $0 to $100 (scales with list) |
| Paid ads | Meta Ads Manager + TikTok Ads Manager | Free (you pay platforms, not tools) |
| Analytics | Google Analytics 4 + Shopify Analytics | Free |
| Creator outreach | Modash Free + spreadsheet | Free |
| Attribution | Post-purchase survey via [KnoCommerce](https://knocommerce.com/) or [Fairing](https://fairing.co/) | $25 to $80 |
| Creative production | Ochoproductions AI stack | Internal |

Total tooling cost in first 6 months: roughly $100 to $300 AUD/month. The expensive line items (Triple Whale, GRIN, paid attribution) come later once spend justifies them.

## Part 7: How to structure a campaign

### Always-on vs campaign vs launch

Three operating modes a brand runs in:

1. **Always-on.** Continuous content, paid amplification, and email flows that run regardless of any specific event. The baseline that keeps a brand alive between big moments.
2. **Campaign.** A defined 2 to 6 week push around a theme (a holiday, a new product drop, a content series). Has a beginning, middle, end, and concrete KPIs.
3. **Launch.** The biggest single moment in a brand's life. Coordinated effort across all channels in a tight window. Usually 3 to 6 months of pre-launch followed by an intense 2 to 4 week launch window.

Sportif's situation right now is "pre-launch into launch." Different rules apply than for an established brand running always-on plus periodic campaigns.

### The pre-launch / launch / sustain model

For a new DTC brand, the right model is three phases:

#### Phase 1: Pre-launch (3 to 6 months before launch day)

**Goals:**
- Build a waitlist (target: 1,000 to 5,000 email signups).
- Establish brand presence on Instagram and TikTok (target: 1K to 10K followers per platform).
- Validate creative direction (which Reels work, which hooks pull engagement).
- Line up 6 to 12 creator partners ready to post at launch.

**Channels active:** Organic Instagram and TikTok. Email signup landing page. Light retargeting paid ads on Meta to people who visit the page.

**What you're NOT doing yet:** Cold paid acquisition at scale. Bottom-of-funnel sales ads.

#### Phase 2: Launch (2 to 4 week window)

**Goals:**
- Convert waitlist into customers (target: 15 to 30% of waitlist converts in launch week).
- Drive press and organic mentions (target: 3 to 5 organic features in fitness or lifestyle publications).
- Scale paid ads with proven creative (target: ROAS 2x or higher).
- Capture purchase data to seed future audiences (target: 500+ first-time customers in launch week).

**Channels active:** Everything. Paid Meta. Paid TikTok. Creator content all firing. Email blasts to waitlist. Organic content daily. PR push. Founder appearances.

**What success looks like in this phase:** A measurable spike in revenue, brand search volume, and follower growth. NOT immediate profitability. Most launches lose money on a paid-ad basis in week one and break even by month three.

#### Phase 3: Sustain (3 months after launch onward)

**Goals:**
- Move from launch hype into a repeatable acquisition system.
- Build retention: email flows generating 25 to 35% of revenue.
- Diversify creator pipeline beyond the launch cohort.
- Test new products, content formats, channels.

**Channels active:** Always-on paid (smaller daily spend, tighter targeting), creator program, retention email flows, organic content at sustainable cadence (3 to 4 posts per week).

### Channel mix logic by funnel stage

| Stage | Channels that fit |
|---|---|
| **Awareness** | Organic Reels and TikToks. Top-of-funnel paid ads (broad targeting). Creator content. PR. |
| **Consideration** | Retargeting ads. Email welcome flow. Long-form creator content (YouTube). Product detail pages. |
| **Conversion** | Abandoned cart email. Conversion-objective paid ads. Limited-time launch offers. |
| **Retention** | Post-purchase email flow. Loyalty program. New product email campaigns. |
| **Advocacy** | UGC contests. Referral program. Customer community (Facebook Group, Discord). |

### How AI creative stacks across phases

The Ochoproductions AI stack contributes differently at each phase:
- **Pre-launch:** Volume of organic content. Use HyperFrames + HeyGen to generate 5 to 10 organic Reels per week per platform from a single creative brief.
- **Launch:** Variant testing at scale. Generate 20+ paid ad creatives per week, run them against each other, double down on winners.
- **Sustain:** Refresh winners and adapt them to seasonal moments (new colours, new collaborations) without expensive re-shoots.

## Part 8: Applied to Sportif: a launch campaign blueprint

### The phasing for Sportif

Four months from today (2026-05-28) to launch (September 2026) breaks into four working phases:

#### Phase 0: Foundation (Now through mid-June, ~3 weeks)

**What we're doing:** Brand kit, positioning lock, content system setup.

**Concrete deliverables:**
- Lucy's intake questionnaire returned and processed (Lucy responds by ~2026-06-02).
- `clients/sportif/brand.md` populated with positioning, voice, visual identity.
- Three to five competitor analyses run on the brands Lucy names in Q4.

- Meta Pixel and TikTok Pixel installed on the Shopify store.
- Instagram and TikTok accounts claimed, bio'd, link-in-bio set up driving to email signup.
- Facebook Page created (low priority but needed for ad account).

**Budget for this phase:** Tooling only ($50 to $150 AUD total). No paid spend yet.

#### Phase 1: Pre-launch and waitlist building (mid-June through end of August, ~10 weeks)

**Goals:**
- Hit 1,000 to 5,000 email waitlist signups.
- Reach 5,000 followers on Instagram, 3,000 on TikTok.
- Validate which content format and hook style works for Sportif.
- Line up 8 to 12 micro creator partnerships ready for launch.

**Content cadence:**
- 4 to 5 organic Reels per week.
- 4 to 5 TikToks per week.
- 2 to 3 Instagram Feed posts per week.
- Daily Stories.
- One weekly email to waitlist with brand-build content (founder story, product sneak peeks, behind-the-scenes).

**Creator pipeline:**
- Week 1 to 4 of phase: Identify 30 to 50 micro Australian fitness creators in the chosen lane. Use Modash free + manual hashtag research (#pilatesaustralia, #pilatessydney, #functionalfitnessau, etc.).
- Week 4 to 8: Reach out to top 30. Aim for 15 product-gift partnerships, with 8 to 12 likely to post.
- Week 8 to 10: Lock 4 to 6 of those into a small monthly retainer ($200 to $500 AUD/month) as launch ambassadors. The other 6+ stay as gifted product partners.

**Paid spend in this phase:** $500 to $1,500 AUD/month, ONLY retargeting waitlist landing page visitors. NO cold acquisition. The goal of paid here is to convert visitors who didn't sign up the first time, not to drive new traffic.

**Email:**
- Set up Klaviyo welcome flow (3 to 5 emails over 7 days).
- Set up browse abandonment and abandoned cart flows (pre-stocked, ready to fire at launch).
- Send weekly campaign to waitlist starting late June.

**What we're testing:**
- Which hook style pulls signups (founder story vs product reveal vs cultural lane angle).
- Which Reel/TikTok format gets the highest watch time and shares.
- Which creators have audiences that engage with Sportif's vibe.

**KPIs to hit by end of Phase 1 (end of August):**
- Email list: 1,000+ subscribers (stretch: 3,000+).
- Instagram followers: 3,000+.
- TikTok followers: 2,000+.
- 8 to 12 creator partners committed to launch.
- At least 3 proven Reel/TikTok hooks with replicable structure.

#### Phase 2: Launch (September, 4 week window)

**Week 1 (launch week):**
- Launch announcement Reel and TikTok, posted simultaneously across Sportif and all creator partners' accounts.
- Launch email to waitlist: early access window (24 to 48 hours) before public launch.
- Paid ads turn on with proven creative from Phase 1. Mix: 70% prospecting (cold), 30% retargeting.
- Founder-led Instagram Live or TikTok Live walkthrough of the products.
- PR push to Australian fitness and lifestyle publications (Body+Soul, Vogue Australia, Beauticate, Broadsheet, Marie Claire AU).

**Week 2 to 4:**
- Daily paid ad variant testing. Aim for 20+ creative variants in market in week 2. Cull losers, scale winners by week 3.
- Continuous creator content (each retained creator posts 2 to 3 times in launch month).
- Email campaigns 2 to 3 times per week: scarcity offers, customer testimonials, founder updates.
- UGC repost campaign: re-share every customer story to brand channels.

**Budget bands for Phase 2 (one month):**
- **Lean:** $5,000 to $10,000 AUD total ad spend. Heavy on TikTok (cheaper CPMs) and creator content.
- **Mid:** $15,000 to $30,000 AUD total. Adds proper Meta cold prospecting at scale.
- **Scale:** $50,000+ AUD total. Adds YouTube ads, Google Shopping at higher bids, broader PR.

**KPIs for launch month:**
- First-time customers: 500 (lean) to 3,000 (scale).
- ROAS on paid: 1.8x to 2.5x. Below 1.8x means cut spend. Above 2.5x means scale.
- Email-driven revenue share: 30%+ of total launch revenue.
- Followers added: 5,000+ across IG and TikTok combined.

#### Phase 3: Sustain (October onward)

**Operating cadence shifts:**
- Paid spend normalises to a steady weekly budget rather than launch-spike.
- Content cadence holds (4 to 5 Reels and TikToks per week minimum).
- Retention email becomes the largest single revenue contributor by month 3 to 4 post-launch.
- Creator program expands beyond launch cohort. Replace partners who underperformed, add new ones.
- New product drops or seasonal collections every 6 to 8 weeks become the rhythm.

**Sustain KPIs:**
- MER (Marketing Efficiency Ratio, total revenue ÷ total marketing spend) holding above 3x.
- Email driving 25 to 35% of total revenue.
- Repeat-purchase rate climbing month over month (sign that retention flows work).
- LTV:CAC ratio reaching 3:1 by month 6 post-launch.

## Part 9: Staying current

Marketing changes fast. The wrong way to stay current is to drink from a hot-take firehose on LinkedIn. The right way: a small number of trustworthy sources, scanned weekly.

### Newsletters worth subscribing to

- **2PM** by Web Smith. The smartest writing on DTC and commerce in English. Free + paid tiers. https://2pml.com/
- **Lenny's Newsletter.** Product, growth, and the discipline of building things people want. https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/
- **Fitt Insider.** The industry newsletter for fitness and wellness, weekly. https://insider.fitt.co/
- **Marketing Brew.** Daily marketing news, brief and useful. https://www.morningbrew.com/marketing
- **DTC Newsletter** by Web Smith's team. Operator-focused DTC weekly. https://www.dtcnewsletter.com/

### Podcasts worth listening to

- **DTC Pod** (Ramon Berrios, Blaine Bolus). Weekly interviews with operators.
- **Glossy Podcast** (Modern Retail / Digiday). Fashion and lifestyle DTC focus.
- **The Marketing Millennials** (Daniel Murray). Marketing fundamentals, practical and unfussy.

### People worth following

Most platforms work better when you follow individuals, not company accounts. Some operators worth following on LinkedIn or Twitter/X:

- **Web Smith** (2PM). DTC strategy.
- **Nik Sharma** (Sharma Brands). DTC marketing at scale.
- **Jess Cervellon** (Feastables, formerly). Email and SMS.
- **Codie Sanchez.** Operator-mindset on building businesses.
- **Brett Bernath** (Triple Whale). Attribution and analytics.

### What to ignore

- **Most LinkedIn "marketing hot takes."** 95% are rehashed truisms. The smart ones bubble up via the newsletters above anyway.
- **"AI will replace marketers" headlines.** Mostly clickbait. AI changes how we work, not whether we're needed.
- **Single-platform gurus** ("Meta ads are dead," "TikTok is the only thing that matters"). Reality is always a mix.

### Signs your playbook needs updating

- A previously winning ad campaign drops in performance for more than 4 weeks straight, despite refreshing creative.
- Your CPM doubles within a quarter without a clear seasonal explanation.
- Platforms publish new policy or feature announcements relevant to your category (e.g., Meta's 2026 Restricted Health and Wellness updates).
- Your competitors' content style shifts visibly and they grow faster than you.
- A new platform or format reaches 10%+ of your target audience's attention (this is how Instagram Stories, then Reels, then TikTok rose to dominance).

When any of these signals fires, pause and read for two weeks before adjusting strategy. Most marketing fires are quenched by going back to fundamentals (the funnel, the flywheel, the channel mix), not by chasing the latest tool.
